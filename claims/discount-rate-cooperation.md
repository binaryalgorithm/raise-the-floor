# Claim: Floor-Raising Changes the Game-Theoretic Conditions for Cooperation

## Statement

Raising material floors lowers temporal discount rates in affected populations, which expands the set of cooperative equilibria sustainable under the Folk Theorem. This is not a psychological observation about generosity. It is a structural change in the parameters of the social game that makes cooperation rational where it was previously irrational.

This is the framework's novel cross-domain prediction: no single existing theory (Goodhart's Law, cognitive load theory, multi-objective optimization, satisficing) generates it. It requires the synthesis — connecting material security (economics), discount rates (behavioral economics), and cooperative equilibria (game theory) through the dimensional collapse framework.

## Mechanism

1. **Material insecurity increases discount rates.** A person in survival mode discounts the future almost to zero — surviving today dominates everything. This is empirically documented: poverty increases temporal discounting (Haushofer & Fehr, 2014; Tanaka et al., 2010; Lawrance, 1991).

2. **The Folk Theorem requires sufficiently low discount rates.** Cooperation can be sustained as a Nash equilibrium in repeated games only when players value future payoffs enough. When discount rates are too high, the future payoff from cooperation is worth less than the immediate payoff from defection. Cooperation collapses — not from selfishness, but from mathematics.

3. **Low floors make cooperation structurally unsustainable.** When material floors are low, discount rates are high, and the Folk Theorem's conditions are not met. People defect not because they are bad but because cooperation is not a Nash equilibrium given their discount rates.

4. **Raising floors lowers discount rates, enabling cooperation.** As material security increases, discount rates drop. The Folk Theorem's threshold is crossed. Cooperation becomes a sustainable Nash equilibrium. The "affect radius expansion" observed psychologically is the behavioral expression of this game-theoretic shift.

## The Novel Prediction

**Existing theories predict separately:**
- Goodhart's Law: single metrics get gamed (no prediction about cooperation)
- Cognitive load theory: scarcity reduces cognitive bandwidth (no prediction about game-theoretic equilibria)
- Multi-objective optimization: multiple objectives outperform single objectives (no prediction about material floors)
- Satisficing theory: "good enough" outperforms maximizing for bounded reasoners (no prediction about discount rates)

**This framework predicts jointly:**
- Material floor-raising → reduced discount rates → expanded cooperative equilibria → more political will for floor-raising → positive feedback loop

This prediction connects three domains (economics, behavioral psychology, game theory) through a causal chain that no component theory generates alone. It is testable: measure discount rates before and after material interventions, then measure cooperative behavior in social dilemma games with the same populations.

## Key Evidence

- **Poverty increases temporal discounting:** Haushofer & Fehr (2014) review the evidence that economic shocks increase discount rates and reduce patience. Tanaka et al. (2010) find higher discount rates in lower-income Vietnamese households. Lawrance (1991) estimates that poor US households discount the future at roughly double the rate of wealthy households.
- **Cash transfers reduce discounting:** Carvalho et al. (2016) find that financial windfalls temporarily reduce impatience in decision tasks. GiveDirectly cash transfer evaluations show shifts in long-horizon planning behavior.
- **The Folk Theorem is established mathematics:** Aumann & Shapley (1994), Fudenberg & Maskin (1986). Cooperation is sustainable as Nash equilibrium if and only if discount factors exceed a game-specific threshold.
- **The connection is novel:** No published work we are aware of explicitly connects material floor-raising → discount rate reduction → Folk Theorem threshold crossing → expanded cooperative equilibria as a causal chain.

## What Would Falsify This

1. **Material floor-raising does NOT reduce discount rates.** If cash transfers, housing-first programs, or UBI experiments show no change in measured temporal discounting, the first link breaks.
2. **Reduced discount rates do NOT increase cooperative behavior.** If populations with lower discount rates do not cooperate more in social dilemma games (controlling for other variables), the second link breaks.
3. **The positive feedback loop does NOT engage.** If increased cooperation does not translate to more political support for floor-raising (i.e., the virtuous cycle stalls), the dynamic claim fails even if the static links hold.

## Implication for the Framework

This is the claim that distinguishes the framework from a collection of existing theories. If it holds, the framework generates a prediction no component theory makes alone, justifying the synthesis as a theory rather than a vocabulary. If it fails, the framework remains a productive structural analogy but not a unified theory with novel predictive power.

## Status

**Developing.** Each link in the causal chain has independent empirical support. The chain as a whole has not been tested as an integrated prediction. The most efficient test: measure discount rates and cooperative behavior before and after a material intervention (cash transfer, housing-first) in the same population. Budget estimate: under $50,000 using existing experimental infrastructure.
