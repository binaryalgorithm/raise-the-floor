# Claim: Status Migrates to the Next Scarce Thing When Basics Are Universal

## Statement

Humans are status-seeking. This is primate biology and cannot be eliminated. However, what status attaches to is entirely contextual and shifts predictably with scarcity. When a previously scarce resource becomes universally available, status detaches from it and migrates to whatever is scarce next. This migration is automatic and requires no intervention.

## Mechanism

Status is fundamentally about relative position, not absolute possession. When everyone has something, possessing it no longer signals anything. The status drive — which is a deep biological motivator — redirects toward whatever dimension still offers differentiation. This has happened repeatedly throughout history without anyone planning it.

## Key Evidence

- **Historical status migration (observable pattern):**
  - Food scarcity → abundance: A full table was once a status display. In developed nations, no one gains status from having dinner. Status migrated to *type* of food (organic, artisanal, rare).
  - Literacy scarcity → universality: Reading was once an elite marker. Universal literacy moved status to *what* you read, then to what you *produce*.
  - Travel scarcity → accessibility: Having visited another country was extraordinary. Now status attaches to *where* and *how* you travel.
  - Information scarcity → abundance: Knowing things was status. Now *curating and synthesizing* information is status.

- **Microcosm evidence — communities where basics are handled:**
  - **Academic communities:** When salary and job security are roughly equal, status attaches to publications, ideas, intellectual contribution.
  - **Open-source communities:** When no one is paid differentially, status attaches to quality of contributions, projects maintained, problems solved.
  - **Maker/hacker cultures:** Status from what you built, not what you bought.
  - These communities demonstrate that the competitive drive persists and remains productive when basics are universal within the group.

- **Veblen goods and positional spending:** Veblen (1899) documented that consumption patterns are driven by status signaling. Frank (1985, 2007) extended this to show that positional competition is the primary driver of spending above subsistence — confirming that status is relative and migrates with context.

- **The resistance tells the story:** Opposition to universal provision is often framed as fiscal but is structurally positional. If everyone has housing, housing loses value as a status signal even though its functional value is unchanged. This is consistent with status-as-relative-position theory and explains resistance that is economically irrational but positionally rational.

## Implication for the Framework

Status migration is the mechanism that makes "remove the ceiling" work alongside "raise the floor." Universal provision does not eliminate competition or motivation. It redirects the competitive drive from acquiring basics (zero-sum when basics are scarce) toward achievement, creativity, and contribution (positive-sum). A status hierarchy built on "who created the most interesting thing" is structurally healthier than one built on "who can afford healthcare."

## Status

**Evidenced.** The historical pattern is well-documented. The theoretical framework (positional goods, Veblen effects, relative status) is established economics. The microcosm evidence from academic and open-source communities provides contemporary demonstration. No controlled experiment exists at civilizational scale (by definition), but the convergent evidence across history, theory, and microcosms is strong.
