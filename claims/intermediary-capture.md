# Claim: Intermediary Capture Prevents Cost Collapse from Reaching Consumers

## Statement

Cost collapse at the production level does not automatically translate to cost collapse at the consumer level. Between the point where a service becomes cheaper to provide and the point where people actually pay less, there is an intermediary layer — insurance companies, platform monopolies, regulatory bodies, professional licensing organizations, administrative bureaucracies — that captures the savings. The difference between "AI makes healthcare cheaper to provide" and "healthcare actually becomes cheaper for people" is entirely determined by whether intermediaries pass savings through or absorb them.

This is not a market failure in the traditional sense. It is the market working exactly as designed: rational intermediaries with pricing power retain the spread between falling production costs and sticky consumer prices. Without structural intervention, AI-driven cost collapse benefits the intermediary layer, not the people the framework aims to reach.

## Mechanism

1. **Production costs fall, consumer prices don't.** When a new technology reduces the cost of providing a service, the savings accrue first to whoever controls the interface between producer and consumer. If that interface is competitive, savings pass through. If it is concentrated, captured, or structurally protected, savings are retained as margin.
2. **Intermediaries maintain pricing power through opacity.** Insurance companies, PBMs (pharmacy benefit managers), platform algorithms, and administrative systems obscure the actual cost of provision. When consumers cannot see the production cost, they cannot demand the savings.
3. **Regulatory capture locks in intermediation.** Professional licensing bodies, zoning laws, certificate-of-need requirements, and regulatory frameworks designed by incumbents create artificial scarcity that persists even after the underlying service becomes abundant.
4. **Switching costs and lock-in prevent competition.** Even when alternatives exist, the cost of switching — changing insurance plans, leaving a platform ecosystem, navigating a new system — keeps consumers paying captured prices.
5. **The intermediary layer compounds.** Healthcare does not have one intermediary. It has insurers, PBMs, hospital administrators, billing companies, compliance departments, and malpractice systems, each adding a layer. Each layer captures margin independently. The aggregate capture exceeds what any single intermediary could extract.

## Key Evidence

- **US healthcare administration:** Administrative costs consume approximately 34% of total US health expenditure, compared to 12% in Canada's single-payer system (Himmelstein et al., 2020, *Annals of Internal Medicine*). Total US administrative spending exceeds $1.2 trillion annually. This is not care. It is the cost of intermediating care. The same doctors, the same treatments, the same drugs — a third of the spending goes to the layer between provider and patient.
- **Pharmaceutical intermediary capture:** Insulin costs approximately $2-5 per vial to manufacture. In the US, it sells for $300+ per vial. The spread is not captured by the manufacturer alone — pharmacy benefit managers (PBMs), wholesalers, and insurance formulary negotiations each extract margin. The three largest PBMs (CVS Caremark, Express Scripts, OptumRx) control ~80% of the market. When Civica Rx launched biosimilar insulin at $30/vial in 2024, the product was viable — proving the production cost was never the barrier. The intermediary layer was.
- **Digital textbooks:** When textbook publishing shifted to digital, the marginal cost of distribution dropped to near zero. Consumer prices did not. Pearson, McGraw-Hill, and Cengage maintained print-equivalent pricing for ebooks and shifted to subscription models (Cengage Unlimited, Pearson+) that lock students into recurring payments. The technology reduced costs; the oligopoly captured the savings.
- **Platform economics:** Uber and DoorDash reduced the coordination cost of ride-hailing and food delivery through technology. Neither passed the savings to consumers (prices have risen steadily post-VC-subsidy phase) or to workers (driver wages, adjusted for expenses, often fall below minimum wage). The platforms captured the efficiency gains as margin or used them to sustain unprofitable growth. The technology worked. The intermediary captured the value.
- **US broadband pricing:** Americans pay roughly 2-3x what consumers in comparable nations (France, South Korea, Romania) pay for equivalent broadband speeds, despite using the same underlying fiber and routing technology. The difference is market structure: US broadband is dominated by regional monopolies and duopolies (Comcast, Charter, AT&T) with minimal competition. The technology is the same. The intermediary layer — ISP monopoly pricing — is the variable.
- **Regulatory capture maintaining artificial scarcity:** The AMA has historically influenced medical school accreditation to limit the number of new physicians, contributing to physician shortages that maintain high compensation and restrict access. The legal profession has actively resisted AI-powered legal tools that could provide basic legal guidance at low cost, with multiple state bar associations issuing restrictions on AI legal assistance. These are not market dynamics — they are intermediary-controlled supply restrictions that persist independent of production costs.

## Where Intermediary Capture Is Genuinely Complex

Not all intermediation is rent-seeking. Some important distinctions:

- **Insurance pools genuine risk.** Health insurance that spreads catastrophic costs across large populations provides real value — the intermediation itself is the product. The capture problem is when administrative overhead, profit extraction, and coverage denial consume a third of premiums rather than a few percent.
- **Platforms solve real coordination problems.** Uber does reduce the cost of matching riders to drivers. The question is not whether the intermediation has value but whether the value captured is proportionate to the value created — and whether the platform's market power allows it to extract far more than the coordination service is worth.
- **Some regulation protects consumers.** Drug safety testing, building codes, and financial oversight exist because unregulated markets produced genuine harm. The problem is when the regulatory apparatus is captured by incumbents and used to prevent competition rather than protect consumers.
- **Quality assurance has real costs.** Medical licensing ensures minimum competence. The capture occurs when licensing bodies restrict supply far beyond what quality assurance requires.

The honest framing: intermediation exists on a spectrum from pure value-creation to pure rent-seeking, and most real intermediaries are somewhere in between. The claim is not that all intermediation is parasitic. The claim is that intermediaries with structural power will capture cost savings by default, and that this capture is the primary barrier between production-level cost collapse and consumer-level cost collapse.

## Implication for the Framework

The [cost-collapse claim](cost-collapse.md) argues that AI is making floor-raising economically inevitable. This claim identifies why that inevitability is conditional: cost collapse is necessary but not sufficient. If the intermediary layer between AI-driven production and human consumption is left intact, the savings accrue to intermediaries, not to floors.

This is why "the market will handle it" is insufficient as a floor-raising strategy. Markets with captured intermediaries do not pass savings through. The price of insulin did not fall for decades despite the production cost being trivial — not because the market failed, but because the market was working exactly as structured: in favor of the intermediaries who controlled it.

The historical precedent is instructive. Every previous cost-collapse cycle that actually raised floors required structural intervention to break intermediary capture: electricity became a public utility, postal service became public infrastructure, water systems became municipal. In each case, the technology made universal provision cheap. The intermediary layer prevented universal provision until the service was reclassified as infrastructure rather than a market good.

The policy implication: some floor components — healthcare, education, legal access, broadband — may need the same reclassification. Not because markets are inherently bad, but because markets with captured intermediaries do not translate cost collapse into floor-raising. The mechanism matters. If AI makes diagnosis nearly free but the insurance-billing-administrative complex still extracts 34% overhead, the floor does not rise. The intermediary layer simply becomes more profitable.

## Status

**Evidenced.** Intermediary capture of cost savings is documented across healthcare (administrative cost comparisons), pharmaceuticals (insulin pricing vs. production cost), education (digital textbook pricing), platform economics (ride-hailing and delivery pricing), and telecommunications (broadband pricing disparities). The historical pattern of breaking intermediary capture through public infrastructure reclassification is established. The specific dynamics of AI-era intermediary capture are developing but follow well-documented structural patterns.
