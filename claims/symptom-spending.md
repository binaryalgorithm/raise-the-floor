# Claim: Current Spending Treats Symptoms, Not Causes

## Statement

The resources to raise material floors already exist within current government spending. The United States spends trillions annually on programs that manage the consequences of low floors rather than raising them. Emergency healthcare instead of preventive care. Incarceration instead of education. Crisis housing instead of stable housing. The spending is enormous because treating symptoms at retail costs orders of magnitude more than solving causes at wholesale.

## Mechanism

Institutional structure creates this pattern:

1. **Compartmentalized budgets:** Healthcare, corrections, housing, education, and emergency services operate on separate ledgers. No single institution sees the aggregate cost of a low floor. Each silo's spending appears locally rational.
2. **Perverse incentives:** Institutions funded to treat symptoms lose funding justification if the symptoms disappear. A hospital system funded by emergency visits has no financial incentive to reduce emergency visits. A corrections system funded per inmate has no financial incentive to reduce incarceration.
3. **Time-horizon mismatch:** Preventive investment pays off over decades. Political and budget cycles operate in 1-4 year windows. The costs of prevention are immediate and attributable. The savings are diffuse and delayed.
4. **Strategic ignorance:** The aggregate picture is unflattering to existing power structures. Keeping costs compartmentalized prevents the full cost of low floors from becoming politically salient.

## Key Evidence

- **Healthcare:** The US spends ~$4.5 trillion/year on healthcare (~17% of GDP), more per capita than any nation, while ranking poorly on life expectancy, infant mortality, and chronic disease outcomes among developed nations. Emergency department visits for conditions treatable by primary care cost 3-10x more per episode.
- **Housing-first programs:** Randomized controlled trials (Tsemberis et al., 2004; At Home/Chez Soi in Canada) consistently show that providing stable housing first — before requiring sobriety or employment — reduces emergency service costs, hospitalization, and incarceration by amounts that offset or exceed the housing cost. Typical findings: $20,000-40,000/year savings per person housed.
- **Early childhood investment:** The Perry Preschool Project and Abecedarian Project (longitudinal studies spanning decades) show 7-to-1 returns on investment through reduced special education, reduced crime, increased earnings and tax revenue. Heckman (2006) formalized this as declining returns to human capital investment with age — the earlier the intervention, the higher the return.
- **Incarceration vs. education:** Average cost of incarcerating one person in the US: ~$40,000-60,000/year (varies by state, some exceeding $100,000). Average cost of a year of community college: ~$10,000. The populations overlap substantially.
- **US mandatory spending:** Social Security, Medicare, Medicaid, and safety net programs constitute roughly $4 trillion/year — the majority of the federal budget. Despite this scale, poverty persists, homelessness increases, and life expectancy declines. The spending manages decline rather than building floors.

## Implication for the Framework

The "we can't afford it" argument is empirically false. The money is already being spent — at much higher cost per outcome than floor-raising alternatives would require. The obstacle is not resources but architecture: spending structured to treat symptoms perpetually rather than resolve causes once.

## Status

**Evidenced.** Each component (healthcare cost inefficiency, housing-first ROI, early childhood returns, incarceration costs) is individually well-documented through peer-reviewed research and government data. The synthesis — that these represent a systemic pattern of symptom-treatment over cause-resolution — follows from the data.
