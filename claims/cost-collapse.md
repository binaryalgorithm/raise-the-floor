# Claim: AI Cost Collapse Makes Floor-Raising Economically Inevitable

## Statement

AI is driving the cost of foundational services (diagnosis, education, legal assistance, administration, information access) toward zero marginal cost. As the cost of providing baseline services collapses, the economic argument against universal provision inverts: it becomes cheaper to provide floors than to manage the consequences of not providing them. This transition does not require political persuasion. It requires only that the cost curves continue their current trajectory.

## Mechanism

1. **AI reduces marginal cost of knowledge work toward zero.** A doctor visit costs $200-500. An AI diagnostic consultation approaches $0. The quality gap is closing; in some domains (radiology, dermatology screening), AI already matches or exceeds average human performance.
2. **Education follows the same curve.** Personalized tutoring — previously available only to the wealthy — is approaching universal availability at near-zero cost. Khan Academy + AI, Duolingo, and similar platforms demonstrate the model.
3. **Legal and administrative access.** Basic legal guidance, form completion, benefits navigation, and administrative tasks that previously required expensive professionals are increasingly automatable.
4. **The inversion point:** When providing a service universally costs less than managing the consequences of not providing it, the economic logic flips regardless of ideology. We are approaching this point for several floor components simultaneously.

## Key Evidence

- **Historical precedent — cost collapse drives universality:** Electricity, clean water, basic telecommunications, and internet access all followed the pattern: expensive luxury → decreasing cost → universal provision becomes cheaper than exclusion. Each transition was resisted by incumbents and eventually became economically inevitable.
- **Current AI cost trajectories:** The cost of AI inference is dropping approximately 10x per year (measured by cost per token across major providers, 2022-2026). Services built on this infrastructure inherit the cost decline.
- **Telemedicine and AI diagnostics:** Studies demonstrate that AI-assisted diagnostics can match specialist-level accuracy at a fraction of the cost. Deployment in low-resource settings is already demonstrating floor-raising potential.
- **Education outcomes with AI tutoring:** Early evidence from AI tutoring studies shows significant learning gains, particularly for students who lack access to human tutors — the students at the lowest educational floor.

## What Remains Uncertain

- **Timeline:** The cost curves are clear but the speed of institutional adoption is not. Regulatory capture, professional licensing, and incumbent resistance can delay deployment significantly.
- **Quality convergence:** AI services approaching human quality is not the same as equaling or exceeding it across all domains. The gap matters for some services more than others.
- **Distribution:** Cost collapse at the production level does not automatically translate to cost collapse at the consumer level. Intermediaries can capture the savings. This is the difference between "AI makes healthcare cheaper to provide" and "healthcare actually becomes cheaper for people."

## Implication for the Framework

AI cost collapse is the mechanism that could make floor-raising economically inevitable rather than politically contested. If the basics become cheap enough, withholding them becomes more expensive than providing them — not as a moral argument but as an accounting one. This does not guarantee the transition happens smoothly or quickly, but it changes the structural incentives that currently maintain low floors.

## Status

**Developing.** The cost trajectories are documented. The historical pattern of cost-collapse-to-universality is established. The specific timeline and institutional dynamics for AI-driven floor-raising remain uncertain. This claim is the most forward-looking in the repository and the most likely to need revision as evidence accumulates.
