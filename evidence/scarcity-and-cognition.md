# Scarcity and Cognitive Bandwidth: The Evidence

## Summary

Material scarcity degrades human cognitive performance through measurable, physiological pathways. Poverty-related financial concerns consume mental bandwidth equivalent to a meaningful fraction of cognitive capacity, chronic stress restructures brain regions responsible for executive function and memory, and these effects compound across the lifespan. The evidence comes from lab experiments, natural experiments, longitudinal studies, and randomized controlled trials across multiple countries. Critically, when material conditions improve, at least some cognitive and psychological deficits reverse — suggesting the damage is not permanent but maintained by ongoing deprivation.

## Key Studies

### Mani, Mullainathan, Shafir, & Zhao (2013) — "Poverty Impedes Cognitive Function"

**Citation:** Mani, A., Mullainathan, S., Shafir, E., & Zhao, J. (2013). Poverty impedes cognitive function. *Science*, 341(6149), 976-980. doi:10.1126/science.1238041

**Methodology:** Two studies. Study 1: Shoppers at a New Jersey mall (n~400) were primed with financial scenarios (cheap vs. expensive car repair) and tested on Raven's Progressive Matrices (fluid intelligence) and a spatial compatibility task (cognitive control). Income was measured and participants were split into lower-income (~$20k) and higher-income (~$70k) groups. Study 2: 464 sugarcane farmers in Tamil Nadu, India, were tested on Raven's Matrices and a numerical Stroop task both before harvest (when poor) and after harvest (when relatively rich) — a within-subject natural experiment exploiting the annual income cycle.

**Key findings:**
- In the mall study, lower-income participants performed significantly worse on both cognitive tasks when primed with hard (expensive) financial scenarios, but performed comparably to higher-income participants on easy (cheap) scenarios. Higher-income participants showed no difference across conditions.
- In the farmer study, the same individuals performed worse on cognitive tasks pre-harvest than post-harvest. The authors reported this difference as comparable to roughly 13 IQ points or losing a full night of sleep. Pre-harvest vs. post-harvest differences in nutrition, time availability, work effort, and physical labor did not explain the effect. Self-reported stress was higher pre-harvest but did not account for the cognitive gap.

**Effect size:** The 13-IQ-point figure (approximately 0.85-0.9 SD) comes from the authors' own framing comparing the magnitude of the experimentally induced effect to known IQ-scale benchmarks. This is the widely cited number, but see limitations below.

**Limitations and debate:** Wicherts & Scholten (2013) reanalyzed the mall study data and argued that the significant interaction was partly an artifact of income dichotomization and ceiling effects on the short cognitive tests. Mani et al. responded that the interaction holds with continuous income measures. Carvalho, Meier, & Wang (2016) found no cognitive changes around paydays among low-income US workers, though the income variation was smaller. The effect has been partially replicated (e.g., in a Colombian sample; Joy 2017 in a US student sample) but has also failed to replicate in other settings (Burlacu et al. 2022; O'Donnell et al. 2021). A 2024 meta-analysis (Almeida et al., *Journal of Economic Psychology*) examining 256 effect sizes from 29 datasets (n=111,852) found a total effect of financial scarcity on cognitive performance of Hedges' *g* = -0.43, but education explained ~60% of the relationship, reducing the partial effect to *g* = -0.15. A separate meta-analysis (Szecsi et al. 2024, *Collabra: Psychology*) examining lab-based scarcity priming studies found a smaller and more ambiguous pooled effect (*g* = 0.06 across 26 effect sizes). The field-based natural experiments (like the farmer study) tend to show larger effects than lab priming studies.

**Bottom line:** The core finding — that financial pressure degrades cognitive performance — is supported, but the magnitude is debated. The 13-IQ-point figure should be treated as an upper bound from a specific natural experiment, not as a universally replicated precise effect size. The meta-analytic evidence suggests a real but more moderate effect in the range of *g* = -0.15 to -0.43, depending on severity of scarcity and controls applied.

### Mullainathan & Shafir (2013) — "Scarcity: Why Having Too Little Means So Much"

**Citation:** Mullainathan, S. & Shafir, E. (2013). *Scarcity: Why Having Too Little Means So Much*. New York: Times Books/Henry Holt.

**Core concept:** The book synthesizes research (including their own experimental work published in Shah, Mullainathan, & Shafir 2012) into the framework of a "bandwidth tax." Scarcity of any kind — money, time, calories, social connection — captures attention involuntarily and reduces the cognitive resources available for everything else. They distinguish two components of bandwidth: fluid intelligence (the capacity to think and reason) and executive control (the capacity to manage impulses and plan).

**Key mechanism — tunneling:** When a person faces scarcity, their attention narrows onto the scarce resource. This produces some benefits (heightened focus on the immediate problem) but at the cost of neglecting other domains. A person tunneling on how to make rent may miss a medical appointment, forget a deadline, or make a poor financial decision in an unrelated domain — not from irresponsibility but from a bandwidth constraint.

**Experimental basis:** Shah, Mullainathan, & Shafir (2012, *Science*) showed that scarcity-induced focus led to overborrowing in experimental games and reduced performance on unrelated cognitive tasks. However, Camerer et al. (2018) failed to replicate the cognitive fatigue result. Shah et al. (2019) conducted high-powered self-replications and found some results were robust while others were less so. A broader empirical audit (Fenneman et al. 2022, *PNAS*) replicated 20 studies from the scarcity literature with "considerable variability in the replicability, including both strong successes and other undeniable failures." The authors disputed aspects of this audit (Shah et al. 2023).

**Bottom line:** The bandwidth tax is a useful conceptual framework. The underlying idea — that preoccupation with unmet needs consumes cognitive resources — is well-supported by the broader literature on cognitive load, attentional capture, and stress. The specific experimental demonstrations have a mixed replication record, which is common in social psychology. The framework is stronger as a synthesis of converging evidence than as a stand-alone experimental finding.

### Evans & Schamberg (2009) — "Childhood Poverty, Chronic Stress, and Adult Working Memory"

**Citation:** Evans, G.W. & Schamberg, M.A. (2009). Childhood poverty, chronic stress, and adult working memory. *Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences*, 106(16), 6545-6549. doi:10.1073/pnas.0811910106

**Methodology:** Prospective longitudinal study following 195 participants from age 9 to age 17. Childhood poverty was measured by income-to-needs ratio. Chronic stress was measured by allostatic load — a composite index of cumulative physiological wear incorporating cardiovascular (blood pressure, heart rate), neuroendocrine (cortisol, epinephrine, norepinephrine), and metabolic (BMI) markers. Working memory was assessed at age 17 using a visuospatial working memory task.

**Key findings:**
- Duration of childhood poverty (number of years spent below the poverty line) significantly predicted reduced working memory capacity in young adulthood.
- This relationship was fully mediated by allostatic load. That is, the pathway ran: more years in poverty -> higher cumulative physiological stress -> worse working memory. When allostatic load was controlled for, the direct relationship between poverty and working memory became non-significant.
- The longer a child lived in poverty, the higher the allostatic load, and the greater the working memory deficit.

**Effect size:** The authors reported that allostatic load fully mediated the poverty-working memory relationship (Sobel test z = 2.13, p < 0.02). The correlation between childhood poverty duration and working memory was significant (specific *r* values are reported in the paper), and the indirect effect through allostatic load accounted for the entirety of this relationship.

**Significance:** This was one of the first studies to identify a specific physiological pathway connecting childhood poverty to adult cognitive deficits. It shifted the conversation from "poverty correlates with worse outcomes" to "here is the biological mechanism by which poverty gets under the skin."

**Limitations:** Modest sample size (n=195). Single measure of working memory. Observational design (poverty was not randomly assigned, so residual confounding is possible despite the mediation analysis). Does not establish that reducing poverty would reverse the effect — only that the correlation exists and is mediated by chronic stress.

### Haushofer & Fehr (2014) — "On the Psychology of Poverty"

**Citation:** Haushofer, J. & Fehr, E. (2014). On the psychology of poverty. *Science*, 344(6186), 862-867. doi:10.1126/science.1232491

**Methodology:** Review article synthesizing experimental and observational evidence on the psychological consequences of poverty, including the authors' own RCT work in Kenya.

**Key findings:**
- Poverty causes elevated cortisol levels, negative affect (unhappiness, worry, anger, depression), and stress. These are not merely correlates — experimental evidence from income shocks (both positive and negative) demonstrates causal effects.
- Stress and negative affect alter economic decision-making: they increase risk aversion and temporal discounting (preference for smaller-sooner over larger-later rewards). Experimentally administered cortisol makes people more impatient and more risk-averse.
- These behavioral changes can perpetuate poverty: risk aversion causes underinvestment in high-return but uncertain opportunities; impatience reduces savings and long-term planning. This creates a feedback loop where poverty produces psychological states that make escape from poverty harder.

**The feedback loop model:**
Poverty -> stress/negative affect -> cortisol elevation -> altered decision-making (more risk-averse, more impatient) -> economic behaviors that perpetuate poverty -> continued poverty.

**Supporting experimental evidence cited:**
- Negative income shocks from drought in Kenya increased both self-reported stress and cortisol levels in farmers.
- An RCT giving $700 unconditional cash transfers (via GiveDirectly) to Kenyan households reduced stress, depression, and cortisol levels for some transfer types.
- Lab studies showing that exogenous cortisol administration increases risk aversion and impatience.

**Limitations:** The feedback loop model is well-supported in its individual links (poverty -> stress, stress -> decision biases, decision biases -> worse economic outcomes) but the full causal chain operating as a self-reinforcing trap has not been tested as a complete system. Some of the cited lab evidence involves small samples. The review does not claim poverty is solely self-perpetuating through psychological channels — structural barriers remain primary.

### Haushofer & Shapiro (2016) — GiveDirectly Unconditional Cash Transfers in Kenya

**Citation:** Haushofer, J. & Shapiro, J. (2016). The short-term impact of unconditional cash transfers to the poor: Experimental evidence from Kenya. *Quarterly Journal of Economics*, 131(4), 1973-2042. doi:10.1093/qje/qjw025

**Methodology:** Cluster-randomized controlled trial. 1,008 households across 120 villages in rural Kenya. Treatment households received unconditional cash transfers from GiveDirectly. Randomization varied: recipient gender (husband vs. wife), transfer magnitude ($404 PPP vs. $1,525 PPP), and timing (lump sum vs. nine monthly installments). Outcomes measured ~9 months after transfer start.

**Key findings:**
- Psychological well-being improved by 0.26 SD on a composite index including happiness, life satisfaction, stress, and depression.
- Happiness and life satisfaction increased; stress and depression decreased.
- **Cortisol:** No significant overall effect on cortisol levels. However, transfers to female recipients produced significantly lower cortisol compared to male recipients (+0.14 SD on wellbeing index for female recipients, driven by cortisol reduction). Monthly installment recipients showed *higher* cortisol, possibly because difficulty saving small amounts created ongoing financial stress.
- **Cognitive function:** The study did not find significant effects on cognitive function measures, though it did find a modest increase in patience (consistent with the Haushofer & Fehr 2014 model).
- Consumption increased from $158 to $193 PPP monthly. Asset holdings, food security, and housing improved.

**Significance for this framework:** Cash transfers improved psychological well-being and some stress markers, but did not produce measurable cognitive gains in this study. This suggests that short-term income relief may address the psychological/affective layer of the poverty-cognition link more readily than the cognitive layer — or that the cognitive effects require longer duration, earlier intervention, or different measurement.

### Troller-Renfree et al. (2022) — Baby's First Years

**Citation:** Troller-Renfree, S.V., et al. (2022). The impact of a poverty reduction intervention on infant brain activity. *Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences*, 119(5), e2115649119. doi:10.1073/pnas.2115649119

**Methodology:** Randomized controlled trial (Baby's First Years study). 1,000 mothers with incomes below the US federal poverty line were randomized at their child's birth to receive either $333/month or $20/month. Infant brain activity (EEG) was measured at age 1.

**Key findings at age 1:**
- Infants in the high-cash group showed more mid- to high-frequency EEG power (alpha, beta, and gamma bands) compared to the low-cash group (effect sizes 0.17-0.26 SD).
- These EEG patterns have been correlated in prior literature with stronger cognitive, language, and social-emotional development later in childhood.

**Key findings at age 4 (follow-up, 2025):**
- No statistically significant impacts on the four preregistered primary outcomes: language, executive function, social-emotional problems, or high-frequency brain activity.
- The early brain-activity differences observed at age 1 did not translate into measurable cognitive or behavioral improvements by age 4.

**Significance:** This is one of the most rigorous tests of whether cash transfers directly improve child cognitive development. The age-1 findings were encouraging and widely cited. The age-4 null results are important and complicate the narrative. The authors and others have noted that $333/month, while meaningful, does not lift families *out* of poverty — it reduces poverty's severity. The threshold effects may require larger transfers or longer duration.

### Ridley, Rao, Schilbach, & Patel (2020) — "Poverty, Depression, and Anxiety: Causal Evidence and Mechanisms"

**Citation:** Ridley, M., Rao, G., Schilbach, F., & Patel, V. (2020). Poverty, depression, and anxiety: Causal evidence and mechanisms. *Science*, 370(6522), eaay0214. doi:10.1126/science.aay0214

**Methodology:** Systematic review of causal evidence on the bidirectional relationship between poverty and mental illness (depression and anxiety), drawing on RCTs, natural experiments, and quasi-experimental designs.

**Key findings:**
- The relationship between poverty and mental illness is bidirectional and causal in both directions: negative economic shocks cause increases in depression and anxiety; mental illness reduces employment and income.
- Antipoverty programs (including cash transfers) improve mental health outcomes.
- Psychological interventions generate economic gains.
- The bidirectional causation creates a poverty-mental illness trap analogous to the Haushofer & Fehr feedback loop but specifically focused on clinical-level depression and anxiety rather than subclinical stress and decision-making biases.

**Note:** The authors published an erratum in 2024 correcting data entry errors that affected some meta-analytic figures, but stated the corrections did not change any of the paper's conclusions.

## Mechanism: How Scarcity Produces Cognitive Deficit

The evidence supports a multi-level causal pathway from material scarcity to diminished cognitive function:

### 1. Attentional capture (immediate)

Financial concerns involuntarily capture attention, leaving fewer cognitive resources for unrelated tasks. This is the "bandwidth tax" described by Mullainathan & Shafir. It operates in real time — a person worrying about rent has less working memory available for other decisions. This is analogous to the well-documented effects of cognitive load on performance: any persistent background concern degrades the processing available for foreground tasks.

### 2. Chronic stress and cortisol (sustained)

Poverty creates ongoing threat perception. The hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal (HPA) axis responds by maintaining elevated cortisol levels. Chronically elevated cortisol has well-documented effects on brain structure and function (Lupien et al. 2009, *Nature Reviews Neuroscience*; McEwen 2007, *Physiological Reviews*):

- **Prefrontal cortex:** Chronic glucocorticoid exposure causes dendritic retraction in medial prefrontal cortex pyramidal neurons. This region governs working memory, planning, impulse control, and decision-making. Structural degradation here manifests as reduced executive function.
- **Hippocampus:** Chronic cortisol suppresses neurogenesis, alters receptor binding, and reduces hippocampal volume. The hippocampus is essential for memory consolidation and spatial reasoning. Impairment here manifests as memory deficits.
- **Amygdala:** Unlike the prefrontal cortex and hippocampus, the amygdala shows dendritic *growth* under chronic stress — becoming more reactive, not less. This tips the balance further toward threat detection and away from executive control.

The net effect is a brain that is structurally biased toward vigilance and away from the deliberative processing needed for long-term planning, complex reasoning, and impulse regulation.

### 3. Allostatic load (cumulative)

Repeated activation of stress response systems creates cumulative physiological wear — allostatic load (McEwen 1998). Evans & Schamberg (2009) demonstrated that this cumulative burden mediates the relationship between childhood poverty duration and adult working memory deficits. The damage is not from any single stressful event but from the relentless accumulation of chronic, low-grade stress without adequate recovery.

### 4. Decision-making feedback loops (behavioral)

The cortisol-driven shift toward risk aversion and temporal discounting (Haushofer & Fehr 2014) produces economic behaviors — underinvestment, undersaving, overborrowing — that maintain poverty. This creates a self-reinforcing cycle: poverty degrades the cognitive and affective systems needed to escape poverty.

### 5. Developmental effects (intergenerational)

Prenatal and early childhood stress exposure programs the HPA axis for heightened reactivity (Lupien et al. 2009). Children raised in poverty develop stress-response systems calibrated for threat-heavy environments. These calibrations persist into adulthood and may transmit intergenerationally through both epigenetic and environmental channels.

## Implications for Floor-Raising

### The cognitive argument for material floors

If poverty is partly a cognitive deficit imposed by environmental conditions — not a fixed trait of the individuals experiencing it — then raising the material floor is not a transfer of resources from productive to unproductive people. It is the removal of a cognitive impairment that suppresses productive capacity. Every person lifted above the scarcity threshold regains bandwidth, executive function, and decision-making quality that insecurity was actively degrading.

### What the evidence supports

1. **The core claim is robust:** Financial scarcity degrades cognitive performance. This is supported by multiple methodologies (lab experiments, natural experiments, longitudinal studies, meta-analyses) across multiple countries. The debate is about magnitude, not existence.

2. **The neurological pathway is established:** Chronic stress -> cortisol -> prefrontal/hippocampal impairment is one of the best-documented pathways in stress neuroscience. Its application to poverty is a straightforward extension.

3. **Relief produces partial but real recovery:** Cash transfers improve psychological well-being and some stress markers. The cognitive recovery evidence is more mixed — the Haushofer & Shapiro RCT did not find cognitive gains, and Baby's First Years showed early brain changes that did not persist to age 4. This may reflect insufficient transfer size, insufficient duration, or the difference between preventing damage and reversing accumulated damage.

4. **Earlier intervention likely matters more:** Evans & Schamberg's finding that cumulative childhood poverty exposure predicts adult deficits suggests that intervening early — raising the floor for families with young children — has outsized returns. Allostatic load accumulates; preventing its accumulation is easier than reversing it.

5. **The feedback loop makes delay costly:** Because poverty's cognitive effects produce behaviors that perpetuate poverty (Haushofer & Fehr 2014; Ridley et al. 2020), each year of inaction allows the cycle to compound. Floor-raising is not a static intervention — it breaks a dynamic trap.

### What the evidence does not support

- **A precise, universal effect size.** The 13-IQ-point figure from Mani et al. is a specific finding from a specific context. Meta-analytic estimates range from small (*g* = -0.15) to medium (*g* = -0.43) depending on methodology and controls. The effect is real but should not be cited as a single fixed number.

- **Immediate, full cognitive recovery from cash transfers.** The evidence for psychological improvement from income relief is stronger than the evidence for cognitive improvement. Structural brain changes from chronic stress may require sustained relief over long periods, especially if the damage accumulated over years.

- **Poverty as purely a cognitive problem.** Structural barriers — discrimination, geographic isolation, institutional exclusion, labor market dynamics — are independent of cognitive function and would persist even if the bandwidth tax were eliminated. The cognitive argument complements structural analysis; it does not replace it.
