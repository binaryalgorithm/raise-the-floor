# The Wage-Productivity Divergence Since the 1970s

## Summary

Since the late 1970s, labor productivity in the United States has grown substantially faster than the compensation of typical workers. The Economic Policy Institute estimates that net productivity rose 59.7% from 1979 to 2019 while median hourly compensation rose only 15.8% -- a gap that represents trillions of dollars in national income redirected away from the broad workforce. The gains flowed instead to top earners and capital owners. This divergence was not an inevitable consequence of technology or globalization; it was shaped by identifiable policy choices, and it is the central evidence that the resources to raise material living standards already exist.

## The Core Data

### What happened

From the end of World War II through the late 1970s, productivity and typical worker compensation grew in rough lockstep. A worker in 1973 earned substantially more in real terms than a worker in 1948, and that increase tracked the economy's expanding output per hour almost one-for-one.

That coupling broke sometime between 1973 and 1979, depending on the measure used. Since then, the two lines have diverged dramatically.

### How large the gap is

The EPI's most cited figures for 1979-2019:

- **Net productivity growth:** 59.7%
- **Median hourly compensation growth:** 15.8%
- **Ratio:** Productivity grew roughly 3.5 times as fast as typical worker pay

Over a longer window (1948-2019), the EPI reports cumulative productivity growth of approximately 253% versus roughly 116% growth in typical worker compensation. The gap accelerated after 1979 and widened further after 2000.

For the period 1979-2013 specifically, the hourly wages of the median worker rose just 6% in real terms -- less than 0.2% per year. The picture improved modestly after 2014 with a tighter labor market, but four decades of divergence had already locked in an enormous cumulative gap.

Using production and nonsupervisory workers (roughly the bottom 80% of earners) rather than median workers, the divergence is even starker: productivity grew roughly eight times faster than pay for this group since 1979.

### The longer view

If the frame is extended back to 1973 (the inflection point most labor economists identify), productivity has roughly doubled to tripled depending on the measure, while median real wages have been approximately flat. The exact magnitudes depend on deflator choice and whether one measures wages or total compensation -- genuine methodological questions addressed in the Debates section below -- but no mainstream measurement erases the divergence entirely.

## Where the Gains Went

The productivity gains that did not flow to typical workers went to two places: higher earners and capital owners.

### Upward redistribution of labor income

The share of total compensation going to the top 10%, and especially the top 1% and top 0.1%, surged after 1979. Piketty and Saez's widely cited data show the top 1%'s share of fiscal income (excluding capital gains) rising from approximately 8% in 1979 to roughly 18% by 2019. Including capital gains, the increase runs from about 10% to 21% over the same period.

EPI's decomposition attributes approximately 19% of the productivity-median compensation gap specifically to the growing inequality of compensation within the labor force -- the disproportionate flow of wage and salary income to the highest earners.

CEO compensation illustrates the pattern in miniature. The ratio of CEO-to-typical-worker compensation rose from roughly 20:1 in 1965 to over 300:1 by the mid-2010s, with the largest increases occurring in the 1990s and 2000s.

### The declining labor share

For most of the postwar period, labor's share of national income hovered around 62-64%. This was stable enough that economists treated it as a stylized fact (one of the "Kaldor facts" of growth theory).

That stability ended. BLS data show the labor share declining from approximately 63% in 2000 to roughly 57% by 2011-2016 -- a drop of about 6 percentage points. Three-fourths of the entire post-1947 decline in labor share occurred between 2000 and 2016. The decline has been attributed to a combination of factors including globalization, automation, rising market concentration, and the weakening of labor institutions.

EPI estimates that the declining labor share (the shift of income from labor to capital) explains roughly 18% of the overall productivity-pay gap.

### Combined effect

Taken together, rising inequality within the labor force and the declining labor share account for the vast majority of the divergence. EPI's most recent decomposition finds that inequality factors together explain 81% of the productivity-median compensation gap over 1979-2019, and 92% of the gap in the 2000-2019 subperiod. The remainder is attributable to the difference between producer and consumer price deflators (discussed under Debates below).

## Policy Drivers

The divergence was not a natural law. It was enabled and amplified by a series of policy choices and institutional changes.

### Union decline

Union membership fell from approximately 27% of the workforce in 1979 to under 12% by 2019, with private-sector unionization dropping from roughly one in three workers in the 1950s to about one in twenty today. This matters beyond the direct effect on union members: the presence of unions historically pressured non-union employers to raise wages and benefits to remain competitive (the "threat effect"). EPI estimates that union decline can explain roughly one-third of the rise in wage inequality among men and one-fifth among women from 1972 to 2007. A separate EPI analysis estimates the decline in union density costs nonunion workers $133 billion annually in lost wages.

### Minimum wage erosion

The federal minimum wage peaked in real purchasing power in 1968 at $1.60 per hour, equivalent to approximately $14-15 in 2025 dollars. It has remained at $7.25 since 2009 -- a real-value decline of over 40% from its 1968 peak. The erosion of the minimum wage floor explains roughly two-thirds of the increase in the wage gap between low-wage and middle-wage workers, according to EPI analysis. Because the minimum wage anchors the bottom of the wage distribution, its erosion has had cascading effects on wages well above the minimum.

### Tax structure changes

Top marginal income tax rates fell from 70% in 1980 to 28% by 1988 under the Reagan-era reforms, and have fluctuated between 35-40% since. Corporate tax rates were cut from 46% to 34% in 1986 and then to 21% in 2017. Capital gains tax rates were similarly reduced. These changes did not directly suppress wages, but they increased the after-tax return to rent-seeking (extracting larger shares of existing income rather than creating new value), making aggressive executive compensation, stock buybacks, and shareholder-first governance more lucrative relative to broadly shared wage increases.

### Deregulation and financialization

Deregulation of finance, transportation, telecommunications, and other sectors -- beginning in the late 1970s and accelerating through the 1980s and 1990s -- shifted bargaining power toward employers and capital owners. The rise of finance as a share of GDP (from roughly 4% in 1970 to 8% by 2010) channeled a growing share of corporate profits into the financial sector, where compensation is highly concentrated at the top.

### Macroeconomic policy orientation

The Federal Reserve's post-1979 focus on inflation control (beginning with the Volcker shock) came at the cost of tolerating higher unemployment. EPI and others have argued that the persistent failure to target full employment over the subsequent decades -- driven by fear that tight labor markets would spark inflation -- was one of the most consequential policy choices suppressing wage growth. The tight labor markets of the late 1990s and 2018-2019 are among the few periods where median wage growth meaningfully accelerated, supporting this interpretation.

### Trade and immigration policy

Expanded trade (NAFTA, China's WTO accession) and immigration increased labor supply competition for lower-skilled workers while providing limited offsetting gains through lower consumer prices. The distributional effects fell disproportionately on workers without college degrees, who saw real wages decline in many cases. (Note: the magnitude of trade's effect on wages is debated; most economists place it as a contributing factor rather than the primary driver.)

## The Counterfactual

What would incomes look like if the divergence had never opened?

### EPI's direct estimate

If median hourly compensation had grown at the same 59.7% rate as net productivity over 1979-2019, the median worker would have earned $32.71 per hour instead of $23.71 -- roughly **$9.00 more per hour**, or approximately **$18,700 more per year** for a full-time worker.

### Broader household income estimates

Median household income in 1973 was approximately $48,000-$53,000 in today's dollars (depending on the deflator). Productivity has grown between 70% and 140% since then, depending on the measure. If median household income had kept pace with even the conservative end of productivity growth, it would be in the range of $82,000-$92,000 rather than the approximately $75,000-$80,000 it actually reached by the early 2020s. Using the higher end of productivity growth estimates, the counterfactual reaches $100,000-$115,000.

The wide range reflects genuine measurement uncertainty (discussed below), but the direction is unambiguous: median households are capturing tens of thousands of dollars less per year than they would have if the postwar relationship between productivity and pay had held.

### The aggregate scale

The gap is not just a per-household figure. It represents a cumulative reallocation of trillions of dollars in national income over four decades. The RAND Corporation estimated in 2020 that the bottom 90% of earners collectively received approximately $2.5 trillion less per year than they would have if income growth had been distributed as evenly as it was from 1945-1974. Over the full 1975-2018 period, the cumulative shortfall was estimated at $47 trillion.

## Debates and Limitations

The divergence is real, but its exact magnitude is contested. Honest engagement with the evidence requires acknowledging several methodological debates.

### The deflator problem

Productivity is conventionally measured using the GDP deflator or the IPD (implicit price deflator), which tracks the prices of goods and services the economy produces. Wages are typically deflated using the CPI or CPI-U-RS, which tracks the prices of goods and services workers consume. These deflators have diverged over time, partly because import prices (which workers pay but the domestic economy does not "produce") rose while the relative price of investment goods fell.

Using the same deflator for both series narrows the gap. Martin Feldstein (2008) argued that using the output deflator for both productivity and compensation eliminates much of the apparent divergence. However, EPI's more recent decomposition finds the deflator difference accounts for only about 19% of the 1979-2019 gap (and just 8% for 2000-2019), with inequality explaining the remainder. The deflator wedge is a real measurement issue, but it does not explain away the divergence -- it explains a modest fraction of it.

### Wages vs. total compensation

Health insurance costs have risen dramatically since the 1970s, and employer-paid benefits are a growing share of total compensation. Critics (notably James Sherk at the Heritage Foundation and Scott Winship at AEI) argue that comparing productivity to wages alone understates worker gains, because rising benefits are part of how workers are compensated.

This is a legitimate point, and EPI's headline figures already use total compensation (wages plus benefits), not wages alone. Even using total compensation, the gap persists. It is true that if one uses *mean* compensation (including top earners' gains) rather than *median* compensation, the gap narrows significantly -- but this is precisely the point. Mean compensation tracking productivity while median compensation lags IS the inequality story.

### The Winship/AEI critique

Scott Winship has argued that when properly measured -- using total compensation, consistent deflators, and including top earners in the average -- worker pay has largely tracked productivity over the past century. His 2024 AEI report represents the most thorough version of this argument.

The critical response is that Winship's framing answers a different question than the one most relevant to typical workers. Showing that *average* compensation tracks productivity is consistent with -- and in fact requires -- a world where gains flow disproportionately to the top, leaving the median behind. The question for policy is not whether total labor income kept up with total output (it roughly did, until the post-2000 labor share decline), but whether the *typical* worker's material standard of living grew in proportion to the economy's capacity. By every measure, it did not.

### Measurement of productivity itself

Some economists argue that productivity growth is overstated in certain sectors due to quality adjustments (e.g., hedonic pricing in technology goods) that may not correspond to real improvements in worker output. Others argue productivity is understated because GDP fails to capture the consumer surplus from free digital services. These are important debates but do not fundamentally alter the distributional story: however large productivity growth "truly" was, typical workers received a shrinking share of it.

### The composition effect

The workforce of 2019 is not the same as the workforce of 1979. Changes in demographics, education levels, industry composition, and hours worked complicate direct comparisons. However, EPI's analysis controls for many of these factors, and the basic divergence persists across most reasonable adjustments.

## International Comparisons

The wage-productivity divergence is not uniquely American, but it is more pronounced in the United States than in most peer economies.

### The OECD picture

In approximately one-third of OECD countries, real median wages have grown at rates similar to or even exceeding labor productivity growth. The decoupling tends to be most severe in countries where productivity growth was around or below the OECD average -- notably the United States, Canada, and Japan.

### Europe

The European experience shows a later and less severe divergence. The European Commission found an "almost perfect one-for-one linkage" between productivity and compensation until 1993, with the first major divergence appearing then and a larger one after 2009. The decoupling has been less pronounced in Europe than in the US or Japan, likely reflecting stronger labor institutions, more robust minimum wage floors (often set by sectoral bargaining), and more comprehensive social insurance systems.

### The institutional interpretation

The cross-country variation supports the policy-choice interpretation. Countries with stronger unions, more centralized wage bargaining, higher minimum wages, and more progressive tax systems have generally experienced less divergence. The gap is not an iron law of capitalist development; it is mediated by institutions and policy.

## Implications

The wage-productivity gap is the single most important piece of evidence for the "raise the floor" thesis: that the material resources to substantially improve baseline living standards already exist within the economy. They are not hypothetical future gains. They are present-day output that is produced but not broadly shared.

### The headroom already exists

If net productivity roughly doubled since 1979 and median compensation grew by only a fraction of that, then the economy is currently producing far more per worker-hour than it is delivering to the typical household. The gap between what the economy generates and what most people receive is not a matter of scarcity -- it is a matter of distribution.

### It was not always this way

The postwar period from 1948 to 1973 demonstrates that broadly shared productivity gains are possible within a market economy. This was not an anomaly or an accident; it was the product of specific institutional arrangements -- strong unions, rising minimum wages, progressive taxation, regulated finance, and macroeconomic policy oriented toward full employment. The divergence that followed was the product of dismantling those arrangements.

### The scale is enormous

Tens of thousands of dollars per median household per year. Trillions of dollars across the workforce annually. The cumulative reallocation over four decades dwarfs the cost of any plausible floor-raising policy. Universal basic income, expanded healthcare, housing subsidies, education funding -- the debate about "how to pay for it" takes on a different character when the money is not missing from the economy but merely misdirected within it.

### This is a floor argument, not a ceiling argument

Nothing here requires capping incomes at the top. The productivity gains are large enough that median incomes could have doubled AND top incomes could have grown substantially. The divergence is not a story about a fixed pie; it is a story about a growing pie where one group captured nearly all the growth. Raising the floor does not require lowering the ceiling -- it requires redirecting a portion of ongoing growth toward the broad base.
