# Evidence: Housing-First Program Outcomes and ROI

## Summary

Housing First — the model of providing permanent housing without preconditions of sobriety or treatment compliance — consistently produces housing retention rates of 80-85% and reduces public service costs through decreased emergency room visits, hospitalizations, shelter use, and incarceration. The strongest evidence comes from multiple randomized controlled trials showing large gains in housing stability, though impacts on substance use and psychiatric symptoms are less clear. The economic case rests not on Housing First being cheap, but on the status quo being enormously expensive: managing homelessness through emergency systems costs $30,000-50,000+ per person per year, while permanent supportive housing costs substantially less and produces better outcomes.

## Key Studies

### 1. Tsemberis et al. (2004) — Pathways to Housing (New York City)

**Citation:** Tsemberis, S., Gulcur, L., & Nakae, M. (2004). Housing First, Consumer Choice, and Harm Reduction for Homeless Individuals With a Dual Diagnosis. *American Journal of Public Health*, 94(4), 651-656.

**Methodology:** Randomized controlled trial. 225 homeless individuals with dual diagnosis (severe mental illness and substance use disorder) in New York City were randomly assigned to Housing First (immediate independent housing with support services, no sobriety requirement) or to treatment-as-usual Continuum of Care programs (housing contingent on treatment compliance and sobriety). Follow-up interviews conducted every 6 months for 24 months.

**Key findings:**
- Housing First group achieved approximately **80% housing retention** over the study period.
- Experimental group obtained housing earlier, remained stably housed at significantly higher rates, and reported higher perceived choice.
- **No difference** in substance use or psychiatric symptoms between groups — Housing First participants did not use more drugs or experience worse mental health outcomes despite having no sobriety requirement.
- Substance abuse treatment utilization was higher in the control group (because it was required), but this did not produce better substance use outcomes.

**Significance:** This was the foundational RCT establishing that chronically homeless individuals with severe mental illness and addiction could maintain independent housing without treatment prerequisites. It challenged the prevailing "housing readiness" model, which held that people needed to demonstrate sobriety and treatment compliance before receiving housing.

### 2. Stefancic & Tsemberis (2007) — Suburban Replication, Four-Year Follow-Up

**Citation:** Stefancic, A., & Tsemberis, S. (2007). Housing First for Long-Term Shelter Dwellers with Psychiatric Disabilities in a Suburban County: A Four-Year Study of Housing Access and Retention. *Journal of Primary Prevention*, 28, 265-279.

**Methodology:** Consumers with severe mental illness and the longest shelter histories in a suburban county were randomly assigned to Housing First or treatment-as-usual. Followed for four years — the longest follow-up of a Housing First RCT at that time.

**Key findings:**
- **Housing retention at 4 years:** 65% for Housing First vs. 22% for treatment-as-usual.
- Demonstrated the model worked outside the original New York City urban context.
- Longer follow-up confirmed durability of housing stability gains.

### 3. At Home/Chez Soi (2009-2013) — Canadian Multi-Site RCT

**Citation:** Goering, P., Veldhuizen, S., Watson, A., et al. (2014). *National At Home/Chez Soi Final Report.* Mental Health Commission of Canada. See also: Latimer, E., et al. (2020). Cost-Effectiveness of Housing First With Assertive Community Treatment. *Psychiatric Services*, 71(12), 1290-1296.

**Methodology:** The largest Housing First RCT ever conducted. Over 2,000 homeless participants with serious mental illness enrolled across five Canadian cities (Vancouver, Winnipeg, Toronto, Montreal, Moncton) between 2009 and 2011. Participants randomized to Housing First (with either Assertive Community Treatment for high-need participants or Intensive Case Management for moderate-need participants) or treatment-as-usual. Primary follow-up of 24 months; extension study tracked outcomes further.

**Key findings — Housing:**
- Housing First participants spent **73% of their time in stable housing** vs. **32% for treatment-as-usual** (high-need group: odds ratio 6.35).
- Among moderate-need participants, 72% were housed all of the time vs. 29% for the control group.

**Key findings — Cost:**
- For high-need participants receiving Housing First with Assertive Community Treatment (ACT): **69% of Housing First program costs were offset** by savings in other services.
- Net annual cost of the intervention after offsets: approximately **CAD $6,311 per person**.
- Cost offsets came from reduced use of: shelters (-$1,943), supportive housing (-$1,793), ambulatory visits (-$4,759), and incarceration (-$1,485).
- For every **$10 invested** in Housing First for high-need participants, **$8.27 was saved** in other service costs.
- For moderate-need participants with Intensive Case Management: 46% of costs were offset.

**Key findings — Health and functioning:**
- Housing First groups showed greater improvements in interviewer-rated community functioning than treatment-as-usual groups.
- Quality of life improvements were observed in the Housing First group.
- As with the Tsemberis studies, **no significant differences** in substance use or psychiatric symptoms between groups.

### 4. Culhane, Metraux, & Hadley (2002) — New York City Service Utilization

**Citation:** Culhane, D. P., Metraux, S., & Hadley, T. (2002). Public Service Reductions Associated with Placement of Homeless Persons with Severe Mental Illness in Supportive Housing. *Housing Policy Debate*, 13(1), 107-163.

**Methodology:** Administrative data linkage study. Analyzed records of 4,679 people placed in supportive housing in New York City between 1989 and 1997, merged with utilization data from public shelters, hospitals, and correctional facilities. Pre-post comparison design (not randomized).

**Key findings:**
- A homeless, mentally ill person on the streets of New York City cost taxpayers **$40,451 per year** (in 1999 dollars) in public services.
- Placement in supportive housing reduced these costs by a net **$16,282 per housing unit per year**.
- Marked reductions in shelter use, hospitalizations, length of stay per hospitalization, and time incarcerated after housing placement.

**Limitation:** Pre-post design without randomization; selection effects possible. However, the large sample size and administrative data (not self-report) strengthen the findings.

### 5. Central Florida Commission on Homelessness (2014) — Cost Study

**Citation:** Central Florida Commission on Homelessness. (2014). *The Cost of Long-Term Homelessness in Central Florida.*

**Methodology:** Examined public service utilization data for 107 chronically homeless individuals in Central Florida, including law enforcement, jail, emergency room, and hospitalization costs.

**Key findings:**
- Each chronically homeless person cost **$31,065 per year** in public services (inpatient hospitalizations, emergency room fees, incarceration, law enforcement).
- Housing with supportive services was estimated to cost **$10,051 per person per year**.
- Housing is approximately **68% less expensive** than leaving people on the streets.

### 6. Denver Supportive Housing Social Impact Bond (2016-2021)

**Citation:** Urban Institute. (2021). *Denver Supportive Housing Social Impact Bond Initiative: Final Evaluation Report.* See also: City and County of Denver. (2021). Independent evaluation report.

**Methodology:** Social impact bond structure. People experiencing chronic homelessness with frequent criminal justice and emergency health system contact were referred to permanent supportive housing. Outcomes compared against a control group receiving usual community services. Evaluated over multiple years.

**Key findings:**
- **77% housing retention** at year 3.
- Annual per-person service costs were **$6,876 lower** for the supportive housing group compared to the control group (against a baseline of $25,554/year for the control group).
- Participants experienced **8 fewer police contacts** and **4 fewer arrests** than those in usual services.
- The city saved an average of **$29,000 per resident** in emergency-related costs.
- Investors received $9.6 million in outcome payments against $8.6 million invested — a positive financial return demonstrating the model could work as a pay-for-success investment.

### 7. Ly & Latimer (2015) — Systematic Review of Cost Offsets

**Citation:** Ly, A., Latimer, E. (2015). Housing First Impact on Costs and Associated Cost Offsets: A Review of the Literature. *Canadian Journal of Psychiatry*, 60(11), 475-487.

**Methodology:** Systematic review of studies examining Housing First costs and offsets, drawing from MEDLINE, PsycINFO, Google, and the Homeless Hub.

**Key findings:**
- Research using rigorous designs **does report cost offsets**, but most studies find **net increases in total costs** for Housing First compared to no intervention.
- The cost argument is frequently overstated: Housing First typically does not "pay for itself" entirely through service savings.
- However, the offsets are substantial — 46-69% of program costs recovered through reduced service utilization in the At Home/Chez Soi trial.
- The appropriate comparison is not "Housing First vs. nothing" but "Housing First vs. what we are currently spending on the same population through emergency systems."

**Significance:** This is an important corrective to the popular narrative. Housing First is not free. It is, however, dramatically more cost-effective than the status quo of cycling people through emergency rooms, shelters, jails, and hospitals.

### 8. Baxter et al. (2019) — BMJ Systematic Review and Meta-Analysis

**Citation:** Baxter, A. J., et al. (2019). Effects of Housing First approaches on health and well-being of adults who are homeless or at risk of homelessness: systematic review and meta-analysis of randomised controlled trials. *Journal of Epidemiology and Community Health*, 73(5), 379-387.

**Methodology:** Systematic review and meta-analysis limited to RCTs. Identified 25 eligible papers from four studies (all in Canada and the US).

**Key findings:**
- **Large improvements in housing stability** — the most robust and consistent finding across all studies.
- **Reduced emergency department visits, fewer hospitalizations, and less time hospitalized** compared to treatment-as-usual, though variability between studies was considerable.
- **No clear differences** in mental health, quality of life, or substance use outcomes in the short term.
- The At Home/Chez Soi trial carried substantial weight in the meta-analysis due to its size.

## Cost Analysis

### What Homelessness Costs (Symptom Management)

The public cost of leaving someone chronically homeless is distributed across systems that do not share ledgers:

| Cost Category | Typical Annual Cost Per Person | Source |
|---|---|---|
| Emergency room visits | $5,000-15,000 | Multiple hospital-level studies |
| Inpatient hospitalization | $10,000-25,000 | Culhane et al. (2002) |
| Incarceration | $5,000-20,000 | Central Florida (2014), Denver SIB |
| Shelter use | $2,000-8,000 | At Home/Chez Soi |
| Police/law enforcement contacts | $2,000-5,000 | Denver SIB, Central Florida |
| **Aggregate per person** | **$30,000-50,000+** | Varies by city and acuity level |

These costs are not hypothetical. The Culhane (2002) study documented $40,451/year in 1999 dollars. The Central Florida study documented $31,065/year in 2014 dollars. The Denver SIB documented $25,554/year in control group service costs.

### What Housing First Costs (Floor-Raising)

| Component | Typical Annual Cost Per Person | Source |
|---|---|---|
| Rent subsidy | $8,000-15,000 | Varies by market |
| Supportive services (ACT or ICM) | $4,000-12,000 | At Home/Chez Soi |
| **Total Housing First cost** | **$12,000-25,000** | Varies by acuity and city |

### The Net Calculation

The math is not complicated:

- **Status quo cost** (emergency cycling): $30,000-50,000+/year per person
- **Housing First cost**: $12,000-25,000/year per person
- **Net savings per person housed**: Typically in the **$10,000-30,000/year range** after accounting for all Housing First program costs
- **Cost offset rate**: 46-69% of Housing First costs recovered through reduced emergency service use alone (At Home/Chez Soi), with additional savings from reduced hospitalization, incarceration, and shelter use

The commonly cited "$20,000-40,000/year savings" figure reflects the range across studies from different cities with different cost structures and population acuity levels. The Culhane (2002) figure of $16,282 in net savings (1999 dollars) is at the conservative end. The Central Florida finding of approximately $21,000 in net savings ($31,065 minus $10,051) and Denver's $29,000 in emergency-related savings are at the higher end.

### The Critical Nuance (Ly & Latimer, 2015)

Housing First does not "pay for itself" in most rigorous analyses. The cost offsets are real and substantial — but they typically recover 50-70% of program costs, not 100%. The remaining 30-50% is a net new expense.

However, this framing misses the point. The comparison is not "Housing First vs. free." It is "Housing First vs. what we are already spending." The status quo of emergency management is both more expensive per person AND produces worse outcomes. Housing First costs less and works better. That it does not cost zero is not an argument against it.

## Outcomes Beyond Cost

### Housing Stability
The most robust finding across all studies. Housing First consistently produces housing retention rates of 75-85% at 2 years (Tsemberis et al., 2004; At Home/Chez Soi), compared to 20-35% for treatment-as-usual. Even at 4 years, retention remains at 65% (Stefancic & Tsemberis, 2007). This is the outcome where the evidence is strongest and most consistent.

### Healthcare Utilization
Participants in Housing First programs show reduced emergency department visits, fewer hospitalizations, and shorter hospital stays (Baxter et al., 2019; At Home/Chez Soi). This reduction is both a cost driver and a proxy indicator of improved health stability.

### Criminal Justice Contact
The Denver SIB found 8 fewer police contacts and 4 fewer arrests per person. Culhane et al. (2002) found reduced incarceration. These reductions are consistent across studies that measure justice system contact.

### Community Functioning
The At Home/Chez Soi trial found greater improvements in interviewer-rated community functioning for Housing First participants. This includes measures of daily living skills, social relationships, and community integration.

### Mental Health and Substance Use
Here the evidence is **mixed and honest about its limits**. Multiple RCTs (Tsemberis et al., 2004; At Home/Chez Soi; Baxter et al., 2019 meta-analysis) found **no significant differences** between Housing First and treatment-as-usual on psychiatric symptoms or substance use. This means two things simultaneously:
1. Housing First does not make substance use or mental health worse (refuting the "enabling" argument).
2. Housing First does not, by itself, resolve substance use disorders or severe mental illness (it is a housing intervention, not a clinical one).

### Employment
Employment outcomes are less consistently studied and show modest effects. Housing stability is a necessary but not sufficient condition for employment. The evidence supports housing as a platform for other interventions — not a replacement for them.

### Quality of Life
At Home/Chez Soi reported quality of life improvements for Housing First participants. Perceived choice — a sense of autonomy over one's living situation — was significantly higher in Housing First groups (Tsemberis et al., 2004).

## Limitations and Debates

### What Critics Say

**1. "Housing First doesn't address root causes like addiction and mental illness."**
This is partially correct. The RCT evidence consistently shows Housing First does not improve substance use or psychiatric symptoms relative to treatment-as-usual. Housing First was designed as a housing intervention, not a clinical one. Critics like the Cicero Institute and Manhattan Institute argue this makes it insufficient as a comprehensive strategy.

**What the evidence shows in response:** Treatment-as-usual (requiring sobriety first) does not produce better clinical outcomes either — and produces dramatically worse housing outcomes. The relevant comparison is not "Housing First vs. a perfect treatment program" but "Housing First vs. what we are actually doing now." On that comparison, Housing First produces equivalent clinical outcomes and vastly superior housing outcomes.

**2. "The Utah 91% reduction was misleading."**
This criticism is well-founded. Utah claimed a 91% reduction in chronic homelessness by 2015, which received extensive national media coverage. Subsequent analysis revealed that the majority of the decline was attributable to a **change in how chronic homelessness was counted**, not to actual reductions in the homeless population. Between 2010 and 2014, when counting methodology was stable, Point-in-Time counts for chronic homelessness in Utah actually increased by 84%. Only 34 new permanent supportive housing beds were added during the period of the claimed decline.

**What this means for the evidence base:** The Utah case should not be cited as evidence for Housing First effectiveness. It is an example of how a politically convenient narrative outran the data. However, this does not invalidate the RCT evidence from Tsemberis, At Home/Chez Soi, and other controlled studies, which used rigorous methodology and did not rely on Point-in-Time counts.

**3. "Housing First doesn't pay for itself."**
Ly & Latimer (2015) confirmed that most rigorous studies find net cost increases, not savings. The popular claim that "Housing First saves money" is an oversimplification. Housing First costs offset 50-70% of program costs through reduced service utilization — significant, but not full cost recovery.

**What the evidence shows in response:** The relevant comparison is not "Housing First vs. zero cost" but "Housing First vs. current emergency-system spending on the same population." On that comparison, Housing First is substantially cheaper per person while producing better outcomes. The net new expenditure buys dramatically better results than the status quo.

**4. "The RCT evidence base is geographically limited."**
Baxter et al. (2019) noted that the meta-analysis drew from only four studies, all in Canada and the US. The At Home/Chez Soi trial dominated effect estimates due to its size.

**What this means:** The evidence is strong but concentrated. More RCTs in diverse contexts would strengthen the case. Observational studies and quasi-experimental designs from other countries (Finland, Australia, Europe) generally support the findings but lack the methodological rigor of RCTs.

**5. "Housing First may not scale to all homeless populations."**
The strongest evidence is for chronically homeless individuals with severe mental illness — the population Housing First was designed to serve. Evidence for other homeless populations (families, youth, people experiencing short-term homelessness) is less developed.

### What the Evidence Does and Does Not Support

**Strongly supported:**
- Housing First produces dramatically better housing stability than treatment-as-usual (multiple RCTs).
- Housing First reduces emergency service utilization and public costs relative to the status quo (multiple studies across designs).
- Housing First does not worsen substance use or mental health outcomes despite having no sobriety requirements (multiple RCTs).

**Supported but with caveats:**
- Housing First is more cost-effective than emergency-system cycling (consistent across studies, but "paying for itself" is overstated).
- Housing First improves quality of life and community functioning (At Home/Chez Soi, smaller studies).

**Not supported:**
- Housing First resolves substance use disorders or severe mental illness (it does not — and was not designed to).
- Housing First alone is sufficient to end homelessness (structural factors like housing supply, affordability, and income adequacy are upstream determinants).
- Utah's 91% reduction was caused by Housing First (it was primarily a measurement artifact).
