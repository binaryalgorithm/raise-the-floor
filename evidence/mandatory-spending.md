# US Mandatory Spending vs. Outcomes: The Architecture of Expensive Failure

## Summary

The United States spends over $4 trillion per year in mandatory federal outlays alone — Social Security, Medicare, Medicaid, and safety net programs — plus $5.3 trillion in total national health expenditures. This spending dwarfs every other nation on Earth. Yet American poverty persists at roughly double the rate of peer nations, life expectancy lags 3-4 years behind comparable countries, and infant mortality ranks near the bottom of the OECD. This is not a resource problem. It is an architectural one: the money flows overwhelmingly into managing consequences rather than raising floors.

## The Numbers

### Federal Mandatory Spending (FY 2024-2025)

Mandatory outlays totaled approximately **$4.1 trillion** in FY 2024, projected to rise to **$4.5 trillion** in FY 2026. Mandatory spending now constitutes roughly **59-63% of total federal outlays**, depending on how net interest is categorized, and that share is growing — projected to reach 80% of the federal budget by 2036 (CBO).

| Program | FY 2024 Spending | What It Does |
|---------|-----------------|--------------|
| Social Security | ~$1.5 trillion | Retirement, disability, survivors benefits for ~71 million people |
| Medicare | ~$912 billion | Health coverage for ~67 million people age 65+ or with disabilities |
| Medicaid & CHIP | ~$626 billion | Health coverage for low-income individuals, families, children |
| ACA Marketplace Subsidies | ~$130 billion | Premium subsidies for exchange plans |
| Income Security Programs | ~$476 billion | SNAP, TANF, housing assistance, SSI, EITC, child tax credits, unemployment |
| Other Mandatory | ~$450+ billion | Veterans' benefits, federal retirement, agriculture, other |

Total federal spending across all categories reached approximately **$7.0 trillion** in FY 2025 (CBO projection).

### What Is Not Counted Above

The mandatory spending figures above represent only the federal ledger. They do not include:

- State and local government social spending
- Private healthcare spending (which roughly equals public healthcare spending)
- Private charity and nonprofit spending (~$500 billion/year)
- Employer-provided benefits
- Lost economic productivity from poverty, chronic disease, and incarceration

When all public and private health spending is included, the number reaches **$5.3 trillion** in 2024 alone — $15,474 per person, 18% of GDP.

## Healthcare: Spending vs. Outcomes

### The Paradox

The United States spends more on healthcare than any nation in history, by any measure:

- **$5.3 trillion** total national health expenditure in 2024 (CMS), growing 7.2% year-over-year
- **$14,885 per person** (2024), versus an average of ~$7,371 among comparable wealthy nations
- **18% of GDP**, projected to reach 20.3% by 2033
- More than **double** the per-capita spending of the average wealthy OECD country

Yet the outcomes are consistently worse than nations spending half as much:

| Metric | United States | OECD Average | US Rank Among 38 OECD Nations |
|--------|--------------|--------------|-------------------------------|
| Life expectancy at birth | 78.4 years | 81.1 years | 30th |
| Infant mortality | 5.6 per 1,000 | 4.2 per 1,000 | 32nd |
| Maternal mortality | Among highest | Well below US | Near bottom |
| Healthcare spending per capita | $14,885 | ~$7,371 (comparable countries) | 1st |

The gap is not marginal. US life expectancy is **4.1 years below** the comparable country average of 82.5 years. Among Black infants, the US infant mortality rate reaches 10.9 per 1,000 live births — more than five times the rate of the best-performing OECD nations.

### Where the Money Goes

About **90% of the $4.1 trillion in annual healthcare expenditure** goes to managing and treating chronic diseases and mental health conditions (CDC). The chronic disease burden alone includes:

- **Obesity:** Affects 40% of US adults, costing ~$173 billion/year in direct healthcare
- **Diabetes:** Affects 13.2% of adults (with 96 million more prediabetic), costing $413 billion/year including lost productivity
- **Cardiovascular disease:** ~$254 billion in healthcare costs plus ~$168 billion in lost productivity
- **Alzheimer's and dementia:** ~$360 billion in care costs (2024)

An estimated **129 million Americans** have at least one major chronic condition. 42% have two or more.

### What This Reveals

The US healthcare system is architected to treat disease downstream, not prevent it upstream. Administrative costs alone — billing, insurance processing, coverage disputes — consume over **$1,000 per person per year**, approximately five times the average of other wealthy countries. The system is not failing to spend enough. It is spending enormously on the wrong layer of the problem. Prices, not utilization, are the primary driver: Americans use fewer healthcare services than citizens of peer nations but pay dramatically more for each encounter.

## Social Security and the Safety Net

### Social Security: Scale vs. Sufficiency

Social Security is the largest single program in the federal budget at ~$1.5 trillion. It is also the most effective anti-poverty program in existence:

- Without Social Security, **37.6%** of older adults would fall below the poverty line
- With it, **10.3%** do — lifting **16.9 million** older adults above the poverty threshold
- Among older Black adults, the poverty rate would be 49.8% without Social Security
- Among older Latino adults, it would be 45.2%

The average monthly retirement benefit is approximately **$2,013-$2,071** (late 2025/early 2026). This is roughly $24,000-$25,000 per year — modestly above the federal poverty line for an individual ($15,060 in 2024) but well below what most researchers consider a livable income in any US metropolitan area. Social Security succeeds at keeping millions from destitution. It does not raise a floor high enough to eliminate survival-mode thinking.

### Safety Net Programs: Spending vs. Poverty Persistence

| Program | Annual Spending | People Served | People Lifted Out of Poverty |
|---------|----------------|---------------|------------------------------|
| SNAP (food assistance) | ~$100 billion (FY 2024) | ~42 million | ~3.6 million |
| TANF (cash assistance) | ~$37.5 billion (federal + state) | Varies widely by state | Limited — benefits far below poverty line in most states |
| HUD housing assistance | ~$70 billion (FY 2024) | ~5 million households | ~2.1 million people |
| SSI (disability/aged) | ~$60 billion | ~7.4 million | Varies |
| EITC + Child Tax Credit | ~$100+ billion | Tens of millions | ~5-6 million (varies by year and CTC structure) |

Despite this combined spending of over $350 billion per year on means-tested safety net programs:

- The official US poverty rate in 2024 was **10.6%** — 35.9 million people
- The Supplemental Poverty Measure (which accounts for benefits received and necessary expenses) was **12.9%**
- Child poverty remains among the highest in the OECD, with more than **one in five** children income-poor
- TANF maximum benefits range from **$204 to $1,370 per month** depending on the state — in most states, far below the poverty line
- States held **$8 billion in unspent federal TANF funds** at the end of FY 2024 — nearly 60% of the annual federal appropriation

The safety net catches some people some of the time. It does not constitute a floor. It is a patchwork of disconnected programs, each with its own eligibility rules, benefit cliffs, and administrative barriers, producing gaps that millions fall through.

## International Comparison

### Who Spends Less and Gets More

The most striking evidence that the US problem is architectural comes from international comparison:

| Country/Group | Poverty Rate | Public Social Spending (% GDP) | Life Expectancy | Healthcare Spending Per Capita |
|---------------|-------------|-------------------------------|-----------------|-------------------------------|
| United States | ~18% (relative) | ~19-22% | 78.4 years | ~$14,885 |
| Denmark | ~5-6% | ~28-30% | ~81+ years | ~$6,000-7,000 |
| Finland | ~5-6% | ~30% | ~82+ years | ~$5,000-6,000 |
| Norway | ~5-7% | ~25-27% | ~83+ years | ~$8,000-9,000 |
| OECD Average | ~11.4% | ~21% | 81.1 years | ~$7,371 |

Key observations:

- **Nordic countries achieve poverty rates one-third of the US level.** Denmark, Finland, and Iceland hold poverty to 5-7% of the population versus 18% in the US.
- **They spend more as a share of GDP on social programs** — but the US spends more in absolute dollars per capita on healthcare alone than most nations spend on their entire social safety net.
- **US healthcare spending per capita is roughly double** the comparable country average, yet delivers worse outcomes on nearly every population health metric.
- **Administrative overhead** consumes a vastly larger share of US spending. The complexity of means-testing, multiple program bureaucracies, and private insurance administration absorbs resources that peer nations direct toward actual care and benefits.

The US is not under-spending. It is spending more than anyone and getting less, because the architecture routes money through systems designed to manage failure rather than prevent it.

## The Architectural Argument

### Massive Spending + Persistent Poverty = Design Failure

The numbers above tell a single story from multiple angles:

**The United States spends trillions managing the consequences of low floors rather than raising them.**

- $5.3 trillion in healthcare mostly treats chronic diseases that correlate with poverty, food insecurity, housing instability, and lack of preventive care
- $1.5 trillion in Social Security keeps seniors barely above a poverty line that does not reflect actual living costs
- $100 billion in SNAP feeds 42 million people month to month without addressing why they need feeding
- $70 billion in housing assistance reaches only a fraction of those who need it, while housing costs continue to outpace incomes
- $37.5 billion in TANF sits partially unspent while its benefits leave families deep in poverty

This is not a resource failure. The US spends more per capita than virtually any nation on the combined cost of healthcare, social insurance, and safety net programs. The failure is in architecture:

1. **Downstream treatment instead of upstream prevention.** 90% of healthcare dollars go to chronic disease management. The conditions driving that disease — poverty, food deserts, housing instability, lack of preventive care — are addressed by separate, underfunded programs with no coordination.

2. **Means-testing instead of universality.** Each program requires its own eligibility determination, creating administrative costs, coverage gaps, benefit cliffs, and stigma. Countries with universal programs achieve better coverage at lower administrative cost.

3. **Fragmentation across ledgers.** No single institution sees the full cost. The healthcare system does not see the housing system's savings from stable housing. The criminal justice system does not see the education system's returns from early investment. Each silo optimizes locally while the aggregate outcome is catastrophic.

4. **Positional defense disguised as fiscal responsibility.** The US poverty rate could be cut to European levels. The spending already exists. What does not exist is the political architecture to redirect it from consequence management to floor-raising — because those who benefit from the current architecture have structural power over those who bear its costs.

The data does not support the claim that the US cannot afford to raise floors. It demonstrates that the US is already paying the full cost of low floors — and then some — through emergency rooms instead of clinics, prisons instead of schools, disability payments instead of livable wages, and chronic disease management instead of prevention.

The money is there. It is simply being spent on the most expensive possible version of the problem.
