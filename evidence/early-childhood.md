# Early Childhood Investment Returns: The Evidence for Floor-Raising at the Start

## Summary

Decades of longitudinal research demonstrate that high-quality early childhood interventions for disadvantaged children produce returns of 4:1 to 13:1 on public investment, primarily through reduced crime, reduced remedial education, increased earnings, and reduced welfare dependency. The returns decline with the age of intervention -- a pattern formalized by economist James Heckman as the principle that "skills beget skills" and early investments raise the productivity of all later investments. This body of evidence constitutes one of the strongest empirical cases for preventive floor-raising over symptom treatment in any domain of social policy.

## Key Studies

### The HighScope Perry Preschool Project

**Citation:** Schweinhart, L.J. et al. (2005). *The High/Scope Perry Preschool Study Through Age 40.* Ypsilanti, MI: High/Scope Press. Subsequent analyses: Heckman, J.J. et al. (2010). "The Rate of Return to the HighScope Perry Preschool Program." *Journal of Public Economics*, 94(1-2), 114-128. Heckman, J.J. et al. (2024). "Perry Preschool at 50." NBER Working Paper 32972.

**Methodology:** Randomized controlled trial conducted 1962-1967 in Ypsilanti, Michigan. 123 low-income African American children (ages 3-4, IQ 70-85) were randomly assigned to a treatment group (n=58) receiving a high-quality preschool program (2.5 hours/day on weekdays plus weekly home visits for two years) or a control group (n=65) receiving no preschool. Participants have been followed at ages 14, 15, 19, 27, 40, and ~55. There were some documented departures from strict random assignment, a limitation acknowledged in the literature, though subsequent reanalyses have accounted for these.

**Key Findings:**
- **Age 27:** Treatment group had significantly higher earnings, were more likely to own homes (36% vs. 13%), and had fewer arrests. Estimated $7.16 returned to the public per dollar invested (Barnett, 1993).
- **Age 40:** Treatment group had higher employment rates (76% vs. 62%), higher median annual earnings ($20,800 vs. $15,300), significantly fewer lifetime arrests (36% vs. 55% arrested 5+ times), and fewer arrests for violent crimes (32% vs. 48%), property crimes (36% vs. 58%), and drug crimes (14% vs. 34%).
- **Age ~55 (midlife follow-up):** Sustained improvements in cognition (contradicting cognitive fadeout claims), marriage stability, earnings, criminal behavior, and health. However, cumulative earnings from ages 15-55 did not show statistically significant differences between groups at the population level.
- **Intergenerational effects:** Children of original participants were more likely to grow up in two-parent households, less likely to be in special education, 17 percentage points less likely to have been suspended from school, more likely to be high school graduates, and less likely to engage in crime. Effects were strongest among male children of male participants.

**ROI:** Estimates range from 7-10% annual rate of return (Heckman et al., 2010) to $7-17 returned per dollar invested depending on the analysis, discount rate, and which benefits are counted. The most conservative peer-reviewed estimate (Heckman et al., 2010) finds approximately $6 in benefits per dollar invested (s.e. 3.4). Cost per participant: ~$23,500 in 2021 dollars.

**Limitations:** Small sample size (n=123). Single site. All participants were African American children in one Michigan city in the 1960s. Generalizability to other populations and eras is debated. Some departures from random assignment protocol occurred.

### The Carolina Abecedarian Project

**Citation:** Campbell, F.A. et al. (2002). "Early Childhood Education: Young Adult Outcomes from the Abecedarian Project." *Applied Developmental Science*, 6(1), 42-57. Campbell, F.A. et al. (2012). "Adult Outcomes as a Function of an Early Childhood Educational Program: An Abecedarian Project Follow-Up." *Developmental Psychology*, 48(4), 1033-1043.

**Methodology:** Randomized controlled trial initiated in 1972 at the Frank Porter Graham Child Development Institute, University of North Carolina, Chapel Hill. 111 infants from low-income families (98% African American) were randomly assigned to either a full-day, year-round educational childcare program (birth to age 5) or a control group. The program had low teacher-child ratios (1:3 for infants to 1:6 for five-year-olds) and used a systematic curriculum of educational games emphasizing language and cognitive development. Participants followed at ages 12, 15, 21, 30, and into mid-30s. A second randomization at school entry created a 2x2 design testing preschool and school-age interventions separately.

**Key Findings:**
- **Age 21:** Treatment group maintained statistically significant advantages in intellectual test performance and academic test scores in reading and math. More likely to attend a four-year college and less likely to become teen parents.
- **Age 30:** Treatment group was 4 times more likely to have earned a college degree. Worked full-time 75% of the time vs. 53% for controls. Six times less likely to require public assistance. However, income-to-needs ratios and criminal involvement did not differ significantly.
- **Health outcomes (mid-30s):** Treated males had lower rates of hypertension, obesity+hypertension clusters, and metabolic syndrome -- an unexpected finding suggesting early cognitive enrichment may influence health trajectories.

**ROI:** Estimated $2.50 returned to taxpayers per dollar spent through higher incomes, reduced educational services, and reduced government costs. Pooled benefit-cost estimate of 6.3:1. Per-child cost: approximately $67,000 total (~$13,900/year for five years, 2002 dollars).

**Limitations:** Small sample (n=111). Single site. Like Perry, primarily African American participants in one geographic area. The intervention was far more intensive (birth to age 5, full-day, year-round) and expensive than most real-world programs, raising questions about scalability.

**What distinguishes it from Perry:** Abecedarian started at birth (vs. age 3-4), was full-day year-round (vs. 2.5 hours/day during school year), and ran for five years (vs. two years). It provides evidence that earlier and more intensive intervention produces stronger educational effects, though the economic returns are somewhat weaker than Perry's, possibly because Perry's crime reduction effects (where much of the dollar return concentrates) were larger.

### Nurse-Family Partnership (NFP)

**Citation:** Olds, D.L. et al. (1997). "Long-term Effects of Home Visitation on Maternal Life Course and Child Abuse and Neglect: Fifteen-Year Follow-up of a Randomized Trial." *JAMA*, 278(8), 637-643. Olds, D.L. (2006). "The Nurse-Family Partnership: An Evidence-Based Preventive Intervention." *Infant Mental Health Journal*, 27(1), 5-25. Eckenrode, J. et al. (2010). "Long-term Effects of Prenatal and Infancy Nurse Home Visitation on the Life Course of Youths: 19-Year Follow-up of a Randomized Trial." *Archives of Pediatrics & Adolescent Medicine*, 164(1), 9-15.

**Methodology:** Three consecutive randomized controlled trials conducted over three decades:
- **Elmira, NY (1977):** 400 predominantly white, low-income, first-time mothers in a semi-rural area.
- **Memphis, TN (1987):** 742 low-income, predominantly African American participants in an urban area.
- **Denver, CO (1994):** 735 low-income, predominantly Hispanic participants.

The intervention: trained registered nurses visit first-time, low-income mothers during pregnancy and through the child's second birthday. Visits focus on maternal health, parenting skills, and life planning. Follow-ups at 15 and 19 years.

**Key Findings:**
- **Child abuse and neglect (Elmira, 15-year follow-up):** Nurse-visited mothers had significantly fewer verified reports of child abuse/neglect as perpetrators. Effect was especially strong among the highest-risk subgroup (low-income, unmarried mothers): log incidence of 0.11 vs. 0.53 for controls (p < 0.001).
- **Maternal outcomes:** Fewer subsequent pregnancies, greater workforce participation, reduced welfare dependency, fewer arrests and convictions.
- **Youth outcomes (19-year follow-up):** Children of nurse-visited mothers had fewer arrests, fewer convictions, less substance use, and less risky sexual behavior. Nurse-visited youth were three times as likely to graduate high school with honors.
- **Child development (18-year follow-up):** Improved math achievement, receptive language, working memory, and emotional perception.

**ROI:** An 18-year follow-up found government savings of $17,310 per family in public benefit costs, yielding a net savings of $4,732 per family (2009 dollars). Program cost varies by location: ~$6,000-$9,600 per family. Benefits concentrate among the highest-risk families; for lower-risk populations, the cost-benefit case is weaker.

**Limitations:** Effects are strongest for the highest-risk subgroup (low-income, unmarried, first-time mothers). The Elmira trial produced stronger results than Memphis and Denver on some outcomes, raising questions about whether results depend on population characteristics or program implementation. Replication in real-world scale-up has not consistently matched trial results.

**What distinguishes it:** NFP intervenes prenatally -- before birth -- making it the earliest-starting intervention in this evidence base. It targets the parent-child relationship rather than the child directly, and its effects on child abuse reduction are among the most robust in the prevention literature.

### Head Start

**Citation:** Puma, M. et al. (2010). *Head Start Impact Study: Final Report.* Washington, DC: U.S. Department of Health and Human Services. Puma, M. et al. (2012). *Third Grade Follow-up to the Head Start Impact Study.* OPRE Report 2012-45. Deming, D. (2009). "Early Childhood Intervention and Life-Cycle Skill Development: Evidence from Head Start." *American Economic Journal: Applied Economics*, 1(3), 111-134.

**Methodology:** The Head Start Impact Study (HSIS) was a congressionally mandated randomized controlled trial. In 2002, approximately 4,667 newly entering 3- and 4-year-old children were randomly assigned to Head Start or a control group across 383 Head Start centers nationwide. Follow-up assessments at end of Head Start year, end of kindergarten, end of first grade, and end of third grade.

Separately, Deming (2009) used a sibling-comparison design within the National Longitudinal Survey of Youth (NLSY) to examine long-term outcomes of Head Start attendance among 1970-1986 birth cohorts.

**Key Findings -- The Fadeout Problem:**
- **End of program year:** Positive impacts on school readiness, language, and literacy. Effect sizes of 0.15-0.30 standard deviations.
- **End of first grade:** Nearly all cognitive and achievement gains had faded to statistical insignificance. The final HHS report (2010) stated: "the benefits of access to Head Start at age four are largely absent by 1st grade for the program population as a whole."
- **End of third grade:** No statistically significant differences between Head Start and control groups on any tested cognitive or academic measure.

**Key Findings -- The Long-Term Paradox:**
- **Deming (2009) sibling comparison:** Despite short-term fadeout, Head Start attendees had 8.5 percentage points higher high school graduation rates and 0.23 SD higher scores on an index of adult outcomes compared to siblings who did not attend, suggesting fadeout in test scores does not mean fadeout in life outcomes.
- **Subsequent research** has found Head Start participation associated with long-term improvements in educational attainment, earnings, self-control, self-esteem, and positive parenting -- even when test score gains disappear by third grade.

**The Fadeout Debate:**
- **One interpretation:** Head Start's test score gains vanish because the program is low-quality or too brief. The control group catches up because many control children access other preschool programs.
- **Counter-interpretation:** Test score convergence does not mean the program had no effect. Head Start may develop noncognitive skills (executive function, social-emotional development, attachment to school) that do not show up on standardized tests but drive long-term outcomes. The fadeout may also reflect low-quality K-12 schooling that fails to build on Head Start gains, rather than a failure of Head Start itself.
- **Methodological issue:** 60-70% of control-group children in the HSIS attended some form of preschool or center-based care. The study measured the impact of Head Start versus the available alternative -- not versus no preschool at all. This substantially compressed the measurable effect.

**ROI:** No single consensus estimate. Cost per child is approximately $9,000-$12,000/year (varies by program). Long-term economic analyses (e.g., Ludwig & Phillips, 2008) estimate benefits of $1.50-$2.00 per dollar invested when long-term outcomes are included, but these estimates are debated.

**What Head Start shows:** At scale, with variable quality, short-term cognitive gains fade. But the program may still produce meaningful long-term benefits through noncognitive channels. The lesson is not that early intervention fails at scale, but that program quality and intensity matter enormously. Head Start's variable quality across 383 sites is fundamentally different from the tightly controlled model programs (Perry, Abecedarian).

## The Heckman Curve

### The Core Argument

James Heckman (Nobel laureate in Economics, 2000) formalized the relationship between age of intervention and return on investment in a series of papers beginning in the mid-2000s. The central framework rests on two mechanisms:

1. **Self-productivity:** Skills acquired in one period persist into future periods and raise the stock of skills in the next period. A child who develops strong language skills at age 3 enters school with an advantage that compounds over time.

2. **Dynamic complementarity:** Early investments make later investments more productive. A child with strong foundational skills benefits more from good schooling than a child without them. Conversely, later remediation for a child who missed early development is both more expensive and less effective because the foundation is absent.

These two mechanisms produce a curve: the rate of return to human capital investment is highest in early childhood and declines with age. The "Heckman Curve" depicts this -- a downward-sloping relationship between age of intervention and return per dollar invested.

**Key papers:** Cunha, F. & Heckman, J.J. (2007). "The Technology of Skill Formation." *American Economic Review*, 97(2), 31-47. Heckman, J.J. (2006). "Skill Formation and the Economics of Investing in Disadvantaged Children." *Science*, 312(5782), 1900-1902.

### What the Curve Means for Policy

- Investing $1 in a high-quality program for a disadvantaged infant or toddler yields more human capital than the same $1 invested in a job training program for a disadvantaged adult.
- This does not mean later investments are worthless -- it means they are less efficient, particularly for the most disadvantaged populations.
- Remediation is inherently more expensive than prevention because it must overcome entrenched deficits rather than building on existing foundations.
- The practical implication: front-loading investment toward the earliest years is the highest-return allocation for a given budget.

### Challenges and Critiques

The Heckman Curve has faced serious empirical scrutiny:

- **Rea & Burton (2020)**, "New Evidence on the Heckman Curve," *Journal of Economic Surveys*, 34(2), 241-262, conducted a meta-analysis using benefit-cost ratios from the Washington State Institute for Public Policy. They found "no support for the claim that social policy programs targeted early in the life course have the largest benefit cost ratios, or that on average the benefits of adult programs are less than the cost of the intervention." They found no clear relationship between age of target population and program cost-effectiveness.

- **Heckman's response:** He argued that Rea and Burton misinterpreted the curve -- it is a statement about the rate of return to marginal investment in disadvantaged individuals, not a claim that average program benefit-cost ratios must decline monotonically with age across all program types. He submitted a reply to the journal but later withdrew it.

- **The honest summary:** The theoretical framework (skills beget skills, dynamic complementarity) is well-supported. The specific claim that returns decline smoothly with age is harder to demonstrate empirically because programs at different ages target different populations, address different problems, and measure different outcomes. Comparing a preschool program's ROI to a job training program's ROI is not a clean apples-to-apples test of the curve. What the evidence does show unambiguously: the highest-quality early childhood programs for the most disadvantaged children produce returns that are difficult for any later intervention to match.

## What Works and What Doesn't

### Characteristics of Effective Programs

The programs that produce the strongest long-term returns share identifiable features:

1. **High quality and intensity.** Perry and Abecedarian were research-designed programs with trained staff, low ratios, structured curricula, and rigorous implementation. They are not representative of average childcare.
2. **Targeting the most disadvantaged.** Returns are highest for children from the most deprived backgrounds. Universal programs show smaller per-child effects (though potentially larger aggregate effects).
3. **Parent engagement.** Perry included weekly home visits. NFP is built entirely around the parent-child relationship. Programs that work with parents -- not just children -- tend to produce more durable effects.
4. **Noncognitive development.** Programs that build executive function, self-regulation, and social-emotional skills produce longer-lasting effects than those focused narrowly on cognitive/academic skills. This may explain why Perry (which emphasized active learning and decision-making) produced stronger lifetime effects than programs focused on IQ or test score gains.
5. **Sufficient duration.** Brief, low-dose interventions produce brief, low-dose effects.

### Why Some Programs Show Weak or Null Results

1. **Quality variation at scale.** Head Start operates across thousands of sites with enormous variation in quality. Averaging across high- and low-quality sites suppresses the signal.
2. **Wrong comparison group.** The HSIS compared Head Start to "whatever else was available," and most control children attended some form of preschool. The incremental effect of Head Start over other preschool is smaller than the effect of preschool over nothing.
3. **Fadeout of the wrong measures.** Programs that boost IQ or test scores often see those gains fade. Programs that build noncognitive skills may not show gains on cognitive tests while still producing long-term life improvements.
4. **Insufficient follow-up.** Many programs are evaluated only at the end of the program year or a few years later. The most important effects (on crime, earnings, health) do not manifest for decades.

### The Quality Threshold

The critical distinction is not "early childhood programs work" versus "they don't." It is: **high-quality, intensive, well-targeted early childhood programs produce extraordinary returns; average-quality programs at scale produce modest, mixed, or hard-to-measure returns.** This is not an argument against early childhood investment. It is an argument for investing in quality, not just access.

## Implications for Floor-Raising

### The Core Case

Early childhood investment is the paradigmatic example of the raise-the-floor thesis: preventive investment in human foundations yields far higher returns than treating symptoms later. The evidence shows:

1. **The multiplier is real.** High-quality early programs for disadvantaged children return $4-13 per dollar invested over the life cycle, primarily through reduced crime, reduced remediation, and increased economic participation. No later-stage intervention consistently achieves comparable returns for comparable populations.

2. **The mechanism is biological.** Early childhood is when the brain is most plastic, when foundational neural architecture for executive function, self-regulation, and learning is established. Intervening during this window is not just economically efficient -- it is working with the grain of neurodevelopment. Later interventions must work against entrenched patterns, which is why they cost more and achieve less.

3. **The costs of inaction compound.** A child who enters school without foundational skills requires special education (2-3x the cost of regular education). A teenager who drops out generates lifetime costs in lost earnings, increased welfare, and increased criminal justice involvement. An adult in chronic poverty generates cascading costs across healthcare, corrections, and emergency services. Each stage of failed intervention makes the next stage more expensive and less effective.

4. **Intergenerational transmission is documented.** Perry Preschool's midlife follow-up shows that the children of treated participants -- the second generation -- have better outcomes than the children of control participants. Raising one generation's floor raises the next generation's starting point. This is compound interest applied to human development.

### What This Means in Practice

- **Front-load investment.** Every dollar shifted from remedial programs to high-quality early childhood programs for the most disadvantaged children produces higher returns. This is not theory -- it is what the longitudinal data shows across multiple studies, multiple decades, and multiple populations.
- **Quality over quantity.** Expanding access to low-quality programs will not replicate the results of Perry or Abecedarian. The returns come from high-quality programs: trained staff, low ratios, structured curricula, parent engagement, noncognitive development.
- **Target the deepest disadvantage.** Universal programs have value, but the highest marginal returns are among the most disadvantaged children -- those whose floors are lowest. This is where the gap between current trajectory and potential is widest.
- **Measure the right outcomes over the right timeframe.** Test scores at age 6 are a poor proxy for life outcomes at age 40. The most important returns (crime reduction, earnings, health, intergenerational effects) require decades to manifest. Evaluating programs on short-term cognitive gains will systematically undervalue the programs that matter most.

### The Structural Insight

Early childhood investment is not a social program. It is infrastructure. Just as a road built once serves traffic for decades, a well-built cognitive and socioemotional foundation serves a person -- and their children -- for a lifetime. The failure to invest is not a savings. It is deferred maintenance that compounds into catastrophic cost.

The evidence reviewed here does not merely suggest that early investment is a good idea. It demonstrates, through decades of randomized longitudinal research, that it is one of the highest-return investments available to any society -- and that the failure to make it is one of the most expensive decisions a society can make.
