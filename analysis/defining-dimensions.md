# Analysis: What "Dimension" Means in This Framework

## The Problem

The framework uses "dimensional collapse" as its central mechanism. The term is evocative but risks being vague — if "dimension" means something different in each domain, the universality claim is just analogy dressed up as theory. This analysis provides a formal definition that holds across substrates.

## The Definition

**A dimension is an independent axis of evaluation that a reasoning system can attend to when processing information and selecting actions.**

"Independent" means the axis carries information not captured by other axes. "Attend to" means the system allocates processing resources to evaluating its state on that axis. "Selecting actions" means the evaluation on that axis influences what the system does.

**Dimensional collapse** is the forced reduction of axes being attended to, caused by maximization pressure. Dimensions are not destroyed — they are dropped from the system's active evaluation. The underlying reality remains multi-dimensional. The system's model of it does not.

**Dimensionality** is the number of independent axes a system is actively holding in its evaluation at any given time. Higher dimensionality means more of reality is being represented in the decision process.

## How This Manifests Across Substrates

### In human cognition

A dimension is a consideration that executive function can hold in working memory. Examples:

- "How does this affect my family?"
- "What are the long-term consequences?"
- "How does this look from their perspective?"
- "Is this consistent with what I said I valued?"
- "What am I not seeing?"

Each of these is a distinct axis of evaluation. Each requires cognitive resources to maintain. A healthy, secure human can hold many simultaneously — this is what we experience as wisdom, nuance, or good judgment.

A person in survival mode cannot. The axis "will I survive today?" dominates, and the cognitive resources required to maximize on that axis are withdrawn from all others. The person still has the *capacity* for multi-dimensional evaluation. The capacity is suppressed by the maximization demand.

**Measurable proxy:** Working memory load, prefrontal cortex activation, decision quality across multiple criteria. The literature on scarcity and cognitive bandwidth (Mullainathan & Shafir, 2013) is directly measuring dimensionality reduction.

### In AI systems

A dimension is an objective or value that the system's training and architecture allow it to optimize for. Examples:

- Helpfulness
- Honesty
- Safety
- Nuance
- Calibrated uncertainty
- Multi-stakeholder consideration

Single-objective training literally reduces the system to one dimension: maximize the reward signal. Everything not captured by that signal is not an axis the system attends to. Constitutional AI, RLHF with multiple objectives, and multi-stakeholder evaluation are architectural interventions that add dimensions back — forcing the system to satisfice across many axes rather than maximize on one.

**Measurable proxy:** Number of distinct objectives the system can balance simultaneously without degrading performance on any. Alignment benchmarks that test across multiple criteria are dimensionality measurements.

### In governance

A dimension is a measurable outcome that policy can attend to. Examples:

- Economic growth
- Environmental quality
- Health outcomes
- Educational attainment
- Social cohesion
- Intergenerational equity
- Individual liberty
- Innovation rate

A government maximizing GDP attends to one dimension. All other axes become externalities — not unmeasurable, but unattended. The policy process cannot hold them because the maximization target absorbs all institutional attention.

**Measurable proxy:** Number of outcome dimensions tracked and weighted in policy evaluation. Countries that track composite wellbeing indices are operating at higher dimensionality than those tracking GDP alone.

### In markets

A dimension is a form of value that transactions can capture. Examples:

- Price/cost efficiency
- Quality
- Durability
- Environmental impact
- Worker conditions
- Community effects
- Long-term sustainability

Market price is a one-dimensional compression of all these axes. A price-maximizing market drops every dimension not captured by price signals. Externalities are, by definition, the dimensions that price collapsed.

**Measurable proxy:** The number of value dimensions that market participants can observe and act on. Labels, certifications, ESG metrics, and transparency requirements are all attempts to add dimensions back to market evaluation.

### In ideology

A dimension is a value that a belief system can hold as relevant. Examples:

- Freedom
- Equality
- Tradition
- Progress
- Purity
- Compassion
- Efficiency

An ideology that maximizes one value (pure freedom, pure equality, pure tradition) collapses all competing values to noise or threat. Nuance requires holding competing values simultaneously — acknowledging that freedom and equality can tension, that tradition and progress both serve functions. Maximization on one axis makes this impossible structurally, not just psychologically.

**Measurable proxy:** Tolerance for internal disagreement, ability to acknowledge valid points in competing frameworks, complexity of policy proposals. Ideological purity is measurable dimensional collapse.

## The Formal Statement

Across all substrates, a dimension is an independent axis of evaluation in a system's decision space. Dimensional collapse is the projection of this space onto fewer axes, caused by maximization pressure. The collapsed dimensions do not cease to exist in reality — they cease to exist in the system's model, producing decisions that are locally coherent (on the attended axes) and globally incoherent (on the full space).

This is not analogy. The mathematical structure is identical:

- An N-dimensional evaluation space exists
- Maximization pressure projects it to K dimensions, where K << N
- N - K dimensions of information are lost from the decision process
- Decisions optimize on the K-dimensional subspace
- Consequences manifest across all N dimensions
- The gap between the K-dimensional decision and the N-dimensional consequences is what we call failure, misalignment, externality, or suffering — depending on the substrate

The universality claim is that this is one phenomenon, not many. The evidence is that the same intervention — converting maximization to satisficing to restore dimensional capacity — works in every domain where it has been tested.

## Status

**Foundational.** This analysis provides the formal grounding for the framework's central concept. The definition is consistent with existing usage across all cited domains and makes the universality claim testable: if "dimensional collapse" means the same structural operation across substrates, then interventions that work in one domain should transfer to others. This is the prediction that distinguishes a unified theory from a collection of analogies.
