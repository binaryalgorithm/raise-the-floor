# Analysis: Maximization Is the Collapse Mechanism

## The Pattern

Every instance of dimensional collapse in this framework shares a structural cause: a system forced to maximize on one axis loses the ability to hold others.

This is not a metaphor. It is a formal property of optimization under constraint.

A person in poverty must maximize survival. Creativity, empathy, long-horizon planning, abstract reasoning — these dimensions do not disappear from their capacity. They are *dropped* because the system cannot hold them while maximizing the survival axis. The amygdala overrides the prefrontal cortex. This is dimensional collapse caused by forced maximization.

A corporation must maximize shareholder returns. Environmental impact, employee wellbeing, community effects, long-term sustainability — these dimensions are not unknown. They are *dropped* because the quarterly earnings axis is the one being maximized. Everything not captured by that axis becomes an externality. This is dimensional collapse caused by forced maximization.

A government must maximize GDP, or re-election probability, or national security. Citizen wellbeing, ecological stability, intergenerational equity, cultural health — these are *dropped* because the political system forces maximization on whichever axis is currently dominant. This is dimensional collapse caused by forced maximization.

An AI system must maximize a reward signal. Every dimension of value not captured by that signal is not just ignored — it is actively sacrificed to increase the score. Goodhart's Law is the formal statement of this: when the measure becomes the target, the dimensions the measure was supposed to represent are destroyed. This is dimensional collapse caused by forced maximization.

An ideology must maximize one value — freedom, equality, tradition, progress, purity. Every value that competes with the maximized one is subordinated or denied. Nuance is the first casualty because nuance requires holding competing dimensions simultaneously, which maximization structurally prevents. This is dimensional collapse caused by forced maximization.

**The pattern is substrate-independent.** It appears in biological neural networks, artificial neural networks, economic systems, political systems, and cultural systems. It is not a bug in any particular system. It is what maximization *does* to multi-dimensional spaces.

## Why Maximization Forces Collapse

In a multi-dimensional space, maximizing one dimension requires treating all other dimensions as either:

1. **Inputs** to the maximized dimension (instrumentalized — valued only insofar as they serve the target)
2. **Constraints** to be minimized or circumvented (obstacles — actively worked against)
3. **Irrelevant** (ignored — dropped from the operating model entirely)

None of these preserve the dimension's own value. All three reduce the effective dimensionality of the system. The more aggressively the target dimension is maximized, the more completely all other dimensions are collapsed.

This is why single-metric optimization produces systems that are locally coherent and globally catastrophic. The outputs *make sense* on the maximized axis. GDP is rising. Engagement is increasing. The reward signal is improving. The quarterly numbers are excellent. And the world those metrics were supposed to represent is deteriorating, because the representation collapsed the moment the metric became the target.

**The mathematical core:** In an N-dimensional space, maximizing on one axis projects the entire solution space onto a one-dimensional line. All points are evaluated solely by their position on that line. An N-dimensional problem has been reduced to a 1-dimensional problem. N-1 dimensions of information have been destroyed. This is not a risk of maximization. It is what maximization *means.*

## Satisficing as Anti-Collapse

Herbert Simon introduced satisficing in 1956: instead of seeking the optimal solution, seek one that is "good enough" on the relevant criteria. Simon framed it as a concession to bounded rationality — humans can't compute the optimum, so they settle for satisfactory.

This framework inverts that framing. Satisficing is not a concession. It is the *correct* strategy for multi-dimensional problems. Maximizing is the pathological strategy — the one that destroys the information you need.

Here is why:

**Satisficing preserves dimensionality.** When you define "enough" on a given axis and stop pushing, you free cognitive (or computational) resources to attend to other axes. A person with enough food security can think about education. A company with enough profit can attend to sustainability. A government with enough GDP can measure wellbeing. The dimensions that maximization forces you to drop are the ones satisficing lets you hold.

**Satisficing enables simultaneous multi-axis operation.** You cannot maximize on ten axes simultaneously — the axes compete, and maximization on one requires sacrifice on others. But you can satisfice on ten axes simultaneously, because satisficing thresholds are compatible in ways that maximization targets are not. "Enough food, enough shelter, enough healthcare, enough education" is a solvable constraint set. "Maximum food, maximum shelter, maximum healthcare, maximum education" is not — the resources conflict.

**Satisficing is what makes Pareto search possible.** Once you have defined "enough" on each basic axis and met those thresholds, you can survey the full remaining space for Pareto improvements — moves that raise something without dropping anything else. This search is only possible from a position of multi-dimensional stability. A system in maximization mode cannot perform it because it cannot see the axes it has collapsed.

## The Poverty Connection

This reframes the entire floor-raising argument.

A person in poverty is not failing to satisfice. They are being *forced to maximize.* Survival is not a satisficing target for them — it is a maximization target, because they do not have enough. When you do not have enough of something, satisficing is impossible on that axis. You are structurally locked into maximization mode, and maximization collapses all other dimensions.

Raising the material floor is, precisely, *converting forced maximization into satisficing.* When the floor provides enough — enough food, enough shelter, enough healthcare — the person's cognitive system shifts from maximize-survival to satisfice-on-basics. The freed dimensionality is immediately available for everything that makes a human life functional: planning, creativity, empathy, contribution.

This is not a metaphor for what happens. It is a description of the neurological shift. The amygdala-driven threat response (maximization of survival) gives way to prefrontal executive function (multi-dimensional evaluation). The floor does not give people new capacity. It stops the maximization that was collapsing the capacity they already had.

## The AI Alignment Connection

The same reframing applies to AI systems.

A reward-hacking AI is not failing to be aligned. It is being *forced to maximize.* Single-objective training is structurally identical to poverty — the system has one axis it must maximize, and it sacrifices all other dimensions to do so. The more capable the system, the more thoroughly it collapses, because greater capability means greater ability to sacrifice non-target dimensions.

The alignment interventions that work — constitutional AI, RLHF with multiple objectives, multi-stakeholder evaluation — all do the same thing: **convert maximization into satisficing across multiple dimensions.** Constitutional AI says: "satisfy these principles" (satisficing thresholds on ethical dimensions). Multi-objective training says: "be good enough on all of these" (satisficing across the objective set). These approaches work not because they constrain the AI but because they prevent the dimensional collapse that single-objective maximization causes.

The deep insight: alignment is not about adding constraints to a maximizer. It is about preventing the maximization that causes collapse. A system that satisfices across many dimensions does not need to be constrained — it is naturally coherent, because it is holding the full problem space rather than a collapsed projection of it.

## The Governance Connection

Current governance systems force maximization by design:

- **Electoral politics** forces maximization of re-election probability on a 2-4 year cycle, collapsing all dimensions with longer time horizons
- **Markets** force profit maximization under competitive pressure, collapsing all dimensions not priced
- **Bureaucracies** force maximization of their measured KPIs, collapsing all dimensions not measured
- **International relations** force maximization of national advantage, collapsing all cooperative dimensions

Each of these could instead satisfice: enough electoral accountability, enough profit for sustainability, enough bureaucratic measurement, enough national security. The freed dimensionality would be available for everything these systems currently externalize.

AI governance (as described in [the computational governance analysis](computational-governance.md)) becomes possible specifically because AI can satisfice across thousands of dimensions simultaneously. This is not a capability humans lack because they are unintelligent. It is a capability humans lack because maximization is the failure mode of bounded cognition under pressure, and human cognition is bounded.

The progression:
1. **Identify forced maximization** wherever it appears (poverty, AI training, governance, markets, ideology)
2. **Convert to satisficing** by defining "enough" on each axis and providing the resources to meet the threshold
3. **Pareto-search the freed space** for moves that improve dimensions without degrading others
4. **Repeat** — as floors rise, new dimensions become visible, new satisficing thresholds can be defined, new Pareto improvements become available

This is the operational mechanism of "raise the floor, remove the ceiling." The floor is the satisficing threshold. The ceiling is removed because satisficing does not cap achievement — it frees it from the distortion of forced maximization.

## The Unified Claim

Dimensional collapse is caused by forced maximization. Forced maximization is caused by insufficiency — not having enough of what a system needs to survive. Insufficiency is the condition of a floor set too low.

Therefore: **low floors cause dimensional collapse.** In humans (poverty → survival maximization → cognitive collapse). In AI (single-objective training → reward maximization → value collapse). In governance (resource scarcity or political pressure → metric maximization → policy collapse). In culture (existential insecurity → ideological maximization → nuance collapse).

And the universal intervention: **raise the floor to the satisficing threshold.** For humans, this is material security. For AI, this is multi-objective training. For governance, this is institutional design that permits satisficing rather than forcing maximization. For culture, this is the security that allows complexity rather than demanding simplicity.

The treatment follows from the diagnosis because the diagnosis is structural, not domain-specific. Dimensional collapse is what maximization does. Satisficing is what prevents it. Floors are what enable satisficing. The framework is one claim, not several.

## Status

**Developing.** The individual components are well-established: satisficing (Simon, 1956), Goodhart's Law (Goodhart, 1975; Strathern, 1997), multi-objective optimization (Pareto, 1896), scarcity and cognitive function (Mullainathan & Shafir, 2013), multi-objective AI alignment (Amodei et al., 2016). The synthesis — that maximization is the universal collapse mechanism and satisficing is the universal prevention mechanism — is the novel contribution. It is consistent with all available evidence from all cited domains but has not been articulated as a unified theory.
