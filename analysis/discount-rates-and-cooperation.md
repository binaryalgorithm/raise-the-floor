# Analysis: Discount Rates and the Game Theory of Cooperation

## Summary

The framework claims that raising material floors enables cooperation. The [affect radius claim](../claims/affect-radius.md) documents the psychological mechanism: security expands concern. The [maximization analysis](maximization-as-collapse.md) documents the structural mechanism: floors convert forced maximization into satisficing. This analysis identifies a third mechanism that may be the most formally rigorous of the three: material floors change the discount rate, which changes whether cooperative equilibria are mathematically sustainable.

This is not a restatement of "secure people are nicer." It is a claim about the parameters of the game itself. The Folk Theorem in repeated game theory proves that cooperation can be sustained as a Nash equilibrium --- but only when players value the future enough. Material insecurity destroys future-valuation. Therefore, low floors do not merely make cooperation psychologically harder. They make it game-theoretically impossible.

---

## The Folk Theorem and Discount Rates

### The Setup

In a one-shot Prisoner's Dilemma, defection is the dominant strategy. Both players defect, both get a worse outcome than mutual cooperation, and there is nothing to be done about it. The incentive structure makes cooperation irrational.

In a *repeated* Prisoner's Dilemma, the Folk Theorem (Aumann & Shapley, 1994; Fudenberg & Maskin, 1986) proves something remarkable: any outcome that gives each player more than their minimax payoff can be sustained as a Nash equilibrium, *provided the discount factor is sufficiently high*. In plain language: if players care enough about the future, cooperation becomes self-enforcing. No external authority is needed. The shadow of the future does the work.

The discount factor, typically denoted delta, ranges from 0 to 1. A delta near 1 means the player values future payoffs almost as much as present ones. A delta near 0 means the player values only the immediate round. The Folk Theorem specifies a threshold: cooperation is sustainable if and only if delta exceeds a critical value that depends on the payoff structure of the game.

Below the threshold, cooperation collapses. Not because players are immoral. Because the math does not support it. The expected value of future cooperative payoffs, discounted at the player's rate, falls below the immediate gain from defection. Defection becomes the rational strategy regardless of the player's preferences, values, or character.

### What Discount Rate Actually Measures

In economics, the discount rate is typically modeled as a time preference parameter --- a stable feature of individual psychology. This is misleading. The discount rate is not a personality trait. It is a response variable that changes with material conditions.

A person who does not know where their next meal comes from has an effective discount rate near infinity. The future is irrelevant --- not because they do not care about it, but because they may not survive to reach it. A dollar tomorrow is worth nothing if you starve today. This is not irrationality. It is the correct Bayesian response to a survival probability that decays rapidly with time.

A person with stable housing, reliable income, and healthcare has a low discount rate. The future is reachable. Investments in relationships, reputation, and cooperative institutions have expected payoffs that are not zeroed out by the probability of not surviving to collect.

## The Empirical Link: Poverty Raises Discount Rates

This is not speculative. The relationship between material insecurity and temporal discounting is one of the most replicated findings in behavioral economics.

**Haushofer & Fehr (2014)** reviewed the evidence on poverty and economic decision-making and documented the causal pathway: poverty increases cortisol (chronic stress), which increases temporal discounting, which produces decisions that appear short-sighted but are locally rational given the discount rate. The decisions (consuming rather than saving, choosing certain small rewards over uncertain larger ones, underinvesting in education and preventive health) are precisely the decisions predicted by a high discount rate. They are not cognitive errors. They are optimal responses to a discount rate that material conditions have set.

**Tanaka, Camerer, & Nguyen (2010)** measured discount rates directly across income levels in Vietnam and found that lower income predicts higher discount rates, controlling for education and other confounds. **Pender (1996)** found the same in rural India. **Lawrance (1991)** documented that the poorest quintile in the United States discounts at roughly triple the rate of the wealthiest quintile.

The direction is causal, not merely correlational. **Mani et al. (2013)** --- the same study that established the poverty-cognition link cited throughout this framework --- showed that inducing scarcity mindset experimentally increases temporal discounting. It is not that impatient people are poor. It is that poverty makes people discount the future, because the future is genuinely less certain for them.

**Carvalho, Meier, & Wang (2016)** found that the same individuals discount more heavily before payday than after, demonstrating that the discount rate shifts with temporary changes in resource availability. The person is the same. The material condition changed. The discount rate followed.

## The Formal Connection: Floors Determine Cooperative Equilibria

Combining the Folk Theorem with the poverty-discount rate evidence produces the framework's core game-theoretic claim:

**1. The Folk Theorem establishes that cooperation requires a discount factor above a critical threshold.**

**2. Material insecurity pushes the discount factor below that threshold.**

**3. Therefore: below a certain material floor, cooperative equilibria are not merely unlikely --- they are not Nash equilibria. They cannot be sustained by rational agents regardless of preference or goodwill.**

This reframes the entire cooperation problem. The conventional framing asks: "How do we make people cooperate?" The answers are usually moral (teach them to be good), institutional (create rules that force cooperation), or incentive-based (make cooperation pay). The discount-rate framing asks a different question: "Under what conditions is cooperation a rational equilibrium?" And the answer is: only when discount rates are low enough. Which means: only when material floors are high enough.

A moral education program aimed at people in survival mode is asking them to adopt a strategy that is not a Nash equilibrium given their discount rate. It will fail --- not because the education is wrong but because the game-theoretic conditions for the strategy to be self-sustaining are not met. An institutional enforcement mechanism can force cooperation temporarily, but enforcement has costs, and when the enforcer's attention lapses, agents revert to the strategy that is actually rational given their parameters.

The only intervention that changes the equilibrium structure itself is changing the discount rate. And the only reliable way to change the discount rate is changing the material conditions that determine it.

## The Affect Radius Mechanism, Formalized

The [affect radius claim](../claims/affect-radius.md) documents that material security expands the range of entities a person is capable of caring about. The discount-rate framework provides the formal mechanism behind that observation.

Cooperation with distant others (strangers, future generations, other communities) is a long-horizon strategy. The payoffs are diffuse and delayed. A person with a high discount rate --- meaning the future is heavily discounted --- cannot rationally invest in cooperation with distant others because the discounted expected payoff falls below the immediate cost of cooperation. The affect radius contracts not (only) because the person psychologically cannot care, but because the game-theoretic return on caring, at their discount rate, is negative.

When the floor rises and the discount rate drops, the same cooperative strategies that were irrational become rational. The expected value of investing in long-horizon, multi-stakeholder cooperation crosses from negative to positive. The affect radius "expands" because the set of cooperative relationships whose discounted payoff exceeds their cost has grown.

This explains the Inglehart finding (1977, 1997) in game-theoretic terms. Post-materialist values --- tolerance, environmentalism, concern for future generations --- are not luxuries that wealthy societies adopt for aesthetic reasons. They are cooperative strategies that become Nash equilibria when discount rates fall below the threshold. The shift from survival values to self-expression values is a phase transition in the equilibrium structure of the social game, driven by the aggregate discount rate crossing the Folk Theorem threshold.

## The Virtuous Cycle

The framework describes self-reinforcing dynamics in several places: the [affect radius claim](../claims/affect-radius.md) documents the psychological cycle (security expands concern, expanded concern supports floor-raising, floor-raising produces security). The discount-rate framework formalizes this cycle and reveals why it has the property of a phase transition rather than a linear gradient.

### The Formal Cycle

1. **Higher material floors** reduce material insecurity.
2. **Lower insecurity** reduces discount rates (Haushofer & Fehr, 2014; Carvalho et al., 2016).
3. **Lower discount rates** expand the set of sustainable cooperative equilibria (Folk Theorem).
4. **More cooperation** enables collective action, including political will for maintaining and raising floors.
5. **Political will for floor-raising** produces higher floors.
6. Return to step 1.

This is a positive feedback loop. But it has a critical feature that linear models miss: the **threshold**. The Folk Theorem does not say that cooperation becomes gradually easier as discount rates fall. It says that below the critical delta, cooperation is not an equilibrium at all, and above it, cooperation is an equilibrium. The transition is discontinuous.

This means the cycle does not spin up gradually. It ignites when the aggregate discount rate crosses the threshold. Below the threshold, defection dominates and floors stay low (the downward spiral in the affect radius claim). Above the threshold, cooperation becomes self-sustaining and floors can rise (the upward spiral). The two spirals are separated by a phase boundary, not a continuum.

### The Last-Place Aversion Wrinkle

**Kuziemko, Buell, Reich, & Norton (2014)** documented last-place aversion: people who are just above the bottom of a distribution resist redistribution more than anyone else, because they fear becoming the new last place. This is a discount-rate adjacent phenomenon --- the person near the bottom has a high discount rate specifically about relative position, and the immediate psychic cost of losing their narrow advantage outweighs the diffuse future benefit of a raised floor.

This creates a strategic obstacle. The people whose discount rates are highest (those at the bottom) would benefit most from floor-raising, but the people just above them --- whose discount rates are also high but whose positional anxiety is maximal --- are the most motivated opponents. **Hopkins & Kornienko (2004)** formalized this: when goods have a positional component, increased equality can reduce welfare for those near the bottom of the current distribution because their positional advantage erodes.

The implication: floor-raising strategies that threaten the relative position of the near-bottom will face resistance from precisely the population that a simple model would predict to be the strongest supporters. This resistance is not irrational. It is a rational response to positional incentives under high discount rates. Effective floor-raising must account for it --- either by raising the floor in ways that do not threaten existing relative positions (universal programs rather than targeted ones), or by raising it rapidly enough that the positional dynamic is overwhelmed by the absolute improvement.

## The Bootstrap Problem

The virtuous cycle has an initialization problem. To reach the cooperative equilibrium, you need to raise floors. But raising floors is itself a cooperative act --- it requires collective action, political will, institutional coordination. These are precisely the capabilities that low floors, high discount rates, and collapsed cooperative equilibria have destroyed.

This is a coordination problem, not a paradox. The question is not whether it can be solved but how it has been solved historically.

### Historical Solutions to the Bootstrap

**Crisis-driven transitions.** The [transition analysis](the-transition-problem.md) documents the pattern: Social Security emerged from the Depression, the NHS from the post-war consensus, the New Deal from economic collapse. In game-theoretic terms, crisis changes the payoff structure of the game. When the status quo becomes sufficiently catastrophic, even agents with high discount rates face an immediate payoff from cooperation (because the alternative --- continued crisis --- is worse than the cost of cooperating now). The crisis effectively lowers the critical threshold by making the non-cooperation payoff terrible. It does not lower discount rates. It changes the game so that the existing discount rates are sufficient for cooperation.

**External shocks to the discount rate.** Some interventions lower discount rates directly without requiring prior cooperation. Cash transfers to populations in extreme poverty reduce discount rates measurably (Haushofer & Shapiro, 2016). These transfers can be funded by a small cooperative coalition (a government, an NGO, a wealthy donor) without requiring the cooperation of the recipients. The transfer lowers the recipients' discount rates, which expands their cooperative capacity, which makes broader cooperation possible. The bootstrap is solved by a small initial investment that shifts the parameters.

**Gradual accumulation.** Not all transitions require dramatic initialization. Axelrod (1984) demonstrated in his iterated Prisoner's Dilemma tournaments that cooperative strategies (specifically, Tit-for-Tat) can invade a population of defectors if they begin in clusters. A small group of cooperators who interact primarily with each other can sustain cooperation within the group, grow, and gradually shift the population equilibrium. Translated to the floor-raising context: a community that raises its own floor (through mutual aid, cooperative economics, or local policy) can demonstrate the cooperative equilibrium to neighboring communities and gradually expand it. This is slower than crisis-driven transition but does not require catastrophe.

**Boyd & Richerson (1992)** extended this with cultural group selection: groups that sustain cooperation outcompete groups that do not, and the cooperative norms spread through imitation and migration rather than requiring each group to independently solve the bootstrap. The mechanism is cultural evolution, not individual decision-making. Groups with higher floors and lower discount rates produce more surplus, attract members, and export their norms. The bootstrap is solved at the group level even when it cannot be solved at the individual level.

## The Stability Problem: Maximizers Can Invade

The [maximization analysis](maximization-as-collapse.md) established that satisficing preserves dimensionality while maximizing collapses it. The cooperative equilibrium described here is a satisficing equilibrium --- agents cooperate and accept "good enough" returns rather than maximizing individual payoffs.

But satisficing equilibria face an evolutionary stability problem. In a population of satisficers, a single maximizer can extract outsized returns by defecting while everyone else cooperates. The maximizer's payoff exceeds the cooperators' payoff. If the maximizer's strategy spreads (through imitation, competitive pressure, or cultural transmission), it can invade and destroy the cooperative equilibrium.

This is not hypothetical. It is the standard problem of public goods and commons dilemmas. It is why "greed is good" can dominate even when cooperation produces better aggregate outcomes. The maximizer is a free-rider, and free-riders can invade cooperative equilibria unless the cooperative equilibrium has enforcement mechanisms.

### Why Discount Rates Alone Are Insufficient

Low discount rates make cooperation *possible* (the Folk Theorem ensures it is a Nash equilibrium), but they do not make cooperation *stable against invasion* by maximizers. A cooperator with a low discount rate who is exploited repeatedly by a maximizer will eventually defect --- either because the exploitation has raised their effective discount rate (through material harm) or because they rationally update that cooperation is not being reciprocated.

The Folk Theorem requires not just that players value the future, but that defection is *punishable* --- that other players can and will respond to defection by withdrawing cooperation. Without credible punishment, the cooperative equilibrium unravels even with low discount rates.

### Institutional Design as Enforcement

This is where the framework connects the discount-rate analysis to institutional design. Cooperation at scale requires institutions that:

1. **Make defection visible.** If exploitation and externalization are invisible, they cannot be punished. Transparency requirements, environmental monitoring, financial disclosure, and the kind of [computational governance](computational-governance.md) described elsewhere in the framework all serve this function. They convert hidden defection into visible defection, which is a prerequisite for the punishment strategies that sustain cooperation.

2. **Make defection costly.** Visibility without consequence is insufficient. Institutions must impose costs on defectors --- through regulation, taxation of externalities, reputation systems, or exclusion from cooperative networks. The costs must be large enough that the payoff from defection, net of punishment, falls below the payoff from cooperation. This is the standard mechanism design problem, but the discount-rate framework clarifies why it is so hard: if the defector has a high discount rate (e.g., a corporation under quarterly earnings pressure), even large future punishments are heavily discounted and fail to deter.

3. **Maintain the floor for the enforcers.** Enforcement requires that the enforcers themselves have low enough discount rates to sustain the long-horizon strategy of monitoring and punishing defection. Underfunded regulators, captured agencies, and demoralized public servants are enforcers whose effective discount rates have been raised by institutional neglect. They cannot sustain the enforcement that sustains the cooperative equilibrium. This is a recursive application of the core claim: the floor must be maintained for the institutional actors, not just the population.

The formal statement: **cooperative equilibria sustained by the Folk Theorem are Nash equilibria but not necessarily evolutionarily stable strategies (ESS). Stabilization requires institutional enforcement that makes defection costly enough to resist invasion by maximizers. This enforcement is itself a cooperative endeavor that requires low discount rates to sustain. The floor must support both the cooperators and the enforcers.**

## The Positional Goods Problem

**Hopkins & Kornienko (2004)** formalized a problem that complicates the entire framework: when goods have a positional component (status, relative rank), raising absolute floors can reduce welfare for those whose positional advantage is eroded. In a purely positional game, redistribution is zero-sum --- every gain in relative position for one person is a loss for another.

This matters for the discount-rate analysis because positional competition introduces a second game layered on top of the material one. Even if a person's material discount rate drops (they have stable food, housing, healthcare), their positional discount rate may remain high if they perceive their relative standing as threatened. The game-theoretic conditions for cooperation may be met on the material axis but not on the positional axis.

The framework's response is in the [status migration claim](../claims/status-migration.md) and the [ceiling mechanics claim](../claims/ceiling-mechanics.md): as material floors rise, status markers shift from material goods (which are zero-sum in positional terms) to non-material achievements (creativity, contribution, expertise), which are less zero-sum. If this status migration is real, then floor-raising gradually reduces the positional game's intensity, which reduces the positional discount rate, which further expands the cooperative equilibrium.

But this migration is slow and not guaranteed. In the near term, the positional dynamics documented by Hopkins & Kornienko and the last-place aversion documented by Kuziemko et al. are real obstacles to floor-raising, not merely psychological biases to be educated away. They are rational responses to a game that has a positional dimension. Floor-raising strategies must be designed with this dimension in mind.

## The Unified Mechanism

The framework now has three interlocking mechanisms connecting material floors to cooperation:

1. **Psychological (affect radius):** Security frees cognitive bandwidth, which expands the range of entities a person can attend to. Documented by Inglehart across 100+ societies.

2. **Structural (satisficing):** Floors convert forced maximization to satisficing, which preserves dimensionality. Formalized in the [maximization analysis](maximization-as-collapse.md).

3. **Game-theoretic (discount rates):** Floors lower discount rates, which expands the set of sustainable cooperative equilibria via the Folk Theorem. Documented by Haushofer & Fehr, Tanaka et al., Lawrance, and others.

These are not three independent claims. They are three descriptions of the same process at different levels of analysis:

- At the individual cognitive level, security frees bandwidth (mechanism 1).
- At the structural level, freed bandwidth enables multi-dimensional evaluation (mechanism 2).
- At the strategic level, multi-dimensional evaluation corresponds to low discount rates because a system that can hold long time horizons and multiple stakeholders in its evaluation is, by definition, one that is not collapsing future and distant payoffs to zero (mechanism 3).

The game-theoretic mechanism is the most formally rigorous of the three because it connects to a theorem with precise conditions. The Folk Theorem does not just suggest that cooperation becomes easier. It specifies the exact threshold condition under which cooperation is an equilibrium. This means the discount-rate claim is, in principle, the most testable: measure the discount rates of a population, compare to the Folk Theorem threshold for the relevant game, and predict whether cooperative institutions will be sustained or collapse.

## Implications

**For the framework:** The discount-rate mechanism completes the formal argument. The framework can now say not just that floors enable cooperation (an empirical observation) but *why* (a game-theoretic proof). The Folk Theorem is not controversial. The poverty-discount rate link is not controversial. The bridge between them --- that this is the mechanism connecting material floors to cooperative equilibria --- is the novel contribution.

**For policy:** Interventions that lower discount rates are cooperation interventions. Cash transfers, universal basic services, stable housing programs --- these are not just welfare. They are investments in the discount-rate infrastructure that cooperation requires. The return on these investments is not just the direct benefit to recipients. It is the expansion of the cooperative equilibrium that benefits everyone, including those who did not receive the transfer.

**For the bootstrap problem:** The analysis identifies three paths to initialization (crisis, external shock to discount rates, gradual accumulation) and specifies why each works in game-theoretic terms. This does not solve the bootstrap problem, but it maps the solution space formally.

**For institutional design:** The stability analysis establishes that low discount rates are necessary but not sufficient. Enforcement is required to prevent maximizer invasion. This connects the abstract game theory to concrete institutional design: transparency, accountability, funded enforcement, and protection of the enforcers' own material floors.

**For the alignment parallel:** AI systems do not have discount rates in the human sense, but they have analogous parameters --- how much future reward is weighted relative to present reward in training, how far ahead the system plans, how many time steps influence the current action. The structural claim transfers: an AI system with a very short effective horizon is in the same position as a human with a high discount rate. It cannot sustain cooperative strategies because it does not weight the future consequences that make cooperation self-enforcing. Constitutional training, long-horizon planning, and multi-step consequence evaluation in AI are the analogues of discount-rate reduction in humans.

## Status

**Developing.** The individual components are well-established: the Folk Theorem (Aumann & Shapley, 1994; Fudenberg & Maskin, 1986), poverty and temporal discounting (Haushofer & Fehr, 2014; Lawrance, 1991; Tanaka et al., 2010), last-place aversion (Kuziemko et al., 2014), positional goods (Hopkins & Kornienko, 2004), evolution of cooperation (Axelrod, 1984; Boyd & Richerson, 1992). The synthesis --- that the Folk Theorem threshold, mediated by discount rates, is the formal mechanism connecting material floors to cooperative equilibria --- is the novel contribution. It is consistent with all cited evidence but has not been empirically tested as a unified hypothesis. The specific prediction (that crossing the Folk Theorem threshold should produce discontinuous rather than gradual increases in cooperative behavior) is testable and distinguishes this framework from simpler "security enables cooperation" narratives.
